# The seven-step home walkthrough.

This is the method consultants use when they walk through a home for the first time. The point is not to fix every problem on the first pass. The point is to **see** the home with structured eyes, name the top five issues, and pick one to start with.

Allow about an hour. Take a notebook. Walk slowly.

## Step 1. Stand at the front door, looking in.

The front door is the **mouth of qi**. In design terms, the first six feet of a home set the tone of the whole space. Whatever a visitor sees first is what the home is unconsciously announcing.

Note three things:

- What is the first solid object your eye lands on? (A wall? A mirror? A long corridor?)
- Is the path inward open or blocked?
- Is the light good?

Write what you see. Do not try to fix anything yet.

## Step 2. Find the centre of the floor plan.

Sketch a rough floor plan on paper. Mark the geometric centre.

This is your **tai chi**, the still point from which the eight sectors of the home are measured. Tradition says the tai chi should be kept open and uncluttered. Practically, this is where you want to be able to stand and see a clear line in every direction.

If the centre of your home is a wall, a heavy piece of furniture, or a bathroom, the bagua-style assessments in the next step will be harder to apply cleanly. That is fine. Note it and continue.

## Step 3. Walk the bagua.

Stand at the centre. Face north. The bagua of your home maps the eight compass directions onto eight life areas: career (north), knowledge (north-east), family (east), wealth (south-east), fame (south), relationships (south-west), creativity (west), and travel (north-west).

Walk to each sector. In each, note:

- What is the room used for?
- Does it feel clean and active, or stuck and cluttered?
- What is the dominant element (wood, fire, earth, metal, water)?

You are mapping the home's *current* state, not its ideal state. The gap between the two is where the work is.

## Step 4. Look for sha qi.

**Sha qi** is the traditional term for cutting or aggressive energy. In practical terms, it is the long straight lines, sharp corners pointing at sitting or sleeping positions, and overhead beams that the eye registers as threats even when the brain does not.

Walk through every room. Ask:

- Is anyone sitting or sleeping in the direct line of a corridor, door, or sharp corner?
- Are there exposed beams over the bed or main workspace?
- Are there long straight lines (driveways, fences, neighbouring rooflines) pointing at the front door?

You are looking for the design choices that create low-grade alertness all day.

## Step 5. Check the front door, the bed, and the stove.

These three are the **most consequential positions** in any home. If you only get three things right in feng shui, get these.

For each, ask:

- **Front door.** Is it solid? Does it open fully? Is the area on either side clear? Does the path inward read as inviting?
- **Bed.** Can you see the door from it without turning your head? Is there a solid wall behind the headboard? Is there a heavy beam, mirror, or sharp corner directly above or facing it?
- **Stove.** Is it clean? Are all burners working? Can the cook see the kitchen entrance from where they stand?

Fix problems in these three before anywhere else.

## Step 6. List the top five fixes.

You now have a notebook full of notes. Pick the **five highest-leverage moves** from everything you have observed. High leverage means: small effort, observable effect, no money required.

A useful rule of thumb: prioritise anything that affects sleep, then anything that affects the front door, then anything that affects the kitchen, then anything else.

Write the five down in priority order. Do the first one this week.

## Step 7. Re-walk in a month.

Most feng shui interventions take a few weeks to register. The home (and you) need time to adapt to the change. After about a month, walk through again with the same notebook.

Some of the things on your list will have resolved themselves through the first fix. Some will have moved up the list because you now notice them. Some will have stayed exactly where they were.

That is feng shui as a structured design practice. Look. Pick one. Wait. Look again.
