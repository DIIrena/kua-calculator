# Your storage, {{firstName}}

Every home has directions that pull two ways. Four of your eight are favourable: the {{kuaGroup}} group that the reading treats as supportive for someone with Kua {{kuaNumber}}. The other four are the cautious ones: {{huoHaiDir}}, {{wuGuiDir}}, {{liuShaDir}}, and {{jueMingDir}}. The question most readers ask is what to *do* with those four. The reading offers a clear, unglamorous answer, {{firstName}}, and it is one of the most practical moves in the whole approach: give them to your storage.

## The job the cautious directions do best

Your favourable walls are the ones to keep under the things that hold your body for hours at a time. The bed, so your head points toward {{shengQiDir}}, {{tianYiDir}}, {{yanNianDir}}, or {{fuWeiDir}}. The desk, so your chair turns the same way. Those four walls are scarce. There are only four of them, and a room usually has a window, a door, and a radiator eating into the good ones before you start.

So the reading suggests something efficient. It hands the four cautious directions a job that does not require you to sit or sleep facing them: **holding what you store.** Closets, cupboards, the utility cabinet, the filing drawers, the boxes you keep but rarely open. None of these have a face that needs orienting. A wardrobe does not care which way it looks. That makes the {{huoHaiDir}}, {{wuGuiDir}}, {{liuShaDir}}, and {{jueMingDir}} corners a natural home for them.

The logic runs in both directions. Storage occupies the walls you would not have used for the bed or the desk anyway, and in doing so it frees the supportive walls for the furniture that earns them. You are not wasting the cautious corners. You are giving them the one task that suits them.

## Contained, and behind a closed door

There is a clear instinct about how stored things should sit, and it is the same whether a corner is favourable or cautious: **contained, and out of sight.** A cupboard with its doors shut reads differently from an open shelf spilling its contents into the room. The closed door is the working principle here. It tells the room that whatever is inside has a settled place and is not loose energy moving through the space.

A few rules of thumb to lean on:

- **Doors closed by default.** A wardrobe, a utility cupboard, a pantry: the resting state is shut. An open closet facing into a room, especially a bedroom, reads as day-life intruding on a space that wants to be still.
- **Everything inside has edges.** Boxes, bins, labelled drawers. Loose piles, even inside a cupboard, read as the same stalled quality as loose piles in the open. Containment is not just the door; it is the order behind it.
- **Nothing stored where the body rests.** Keep boxes from under the bed if you can, and storage furniture off your supportive walls. The cautious corners are where this load belongs.

This is the move that lets a cautious corner carry weight without becoming heavy. A {{jueMingDir}} or {{wuGuiDir}} corner full of neatly contained, closed-away storage is doing exactly the job it is suited to. The same corner with an open, overflowing shelf is the version worth changing first.

## Clutter is stalled energy

A simple way to read clutter: it is energy that has stopped moving. Not a moral failing, not bad luck, just qi that arrived somewhere and never left. A hallway used as long-term storage cannot let qi flow through it. A dining table buried under mail is a table the family stops eating at. The diagnostic question is not "is this tidy" but "is anything here stuck."

This is why storage and clutter are the same topic seen from two sides. Storage is the system that *prevents* clutter: it gives every object a contained place, behind a door, in a corner suited to holding rather than living. Clutter is what happens when objects have no such place and settle wherever they land: on the supportive walls, across the desk, in the line of sight from the bed.

So the cautious corners earn their keep twice. They take the storage off your favourable walls, and a working storage system is the thing that keeps the rest of the home from stalling. A reader who gives {{huoHaiDir}}, {{wuGuiDir}}, {{liuShaDir}}, and {{jueMingDir}} each a quiet, contained purpose has usually settled most of their clutter without thinking about clutter at all.

## The fixed sectors, checked against you

Storage also sits on the bagua, the fixed compass map every home shares. One sector sits in the Southeast of any floor plan; another in the Northeast; another in the Northwest. These sectors are the same for everyone, which is why you have to check them against *your own* directions rather than assume what they mean for you.

Take the corner you are thinking of storing in and find its compass sector, then read it two ways:

- **If that sector is one of your four supportive directions** ({{shengQiDir}}, {{tianYiDir}}, {{yanNianDir}}, {{fuWeiDir}}), think twice before filling it with deep storage. A wall that is strong for you is often better spent on the bed, the desk, or an open, active use. Store there only if the room gives you no better option.
- **If that sector is one of your four cautious directions** ({{huoHaiDir}}, {{wuGuiDir}}, {{liuShaDir}}, {{jueMingDir}}), it is a sound place for a contained cupboard. You are using a corner you would handle gently anyway for a task that asks nothing active of it.

No fixed sector tells you which life-quality it "is" for you; that depends on your own directions, which is why you check. What the approach offers is the pairing rule: active uses want your supportive walls, and contained storage is the graceful use for the cautious ones.

## A move to try

Pick the one corner of your home that you most avoid: the overflowing closet, the box room, the cupboard whose door no longer shuts. Find its direction. If it is one of {{huoHaiDir}}, {{wuGuiDir}}, {{liuShaDir}}, or {{jueMingDir}}, you are already half right: the location suits storage. The work is containment. Give everything inside an edge, get the door to close, and let that corner do its quiet job.

Then look at your supportive walls. If a wardrobe or a stack of boxes is sitting on a {{shengQiDir}}, {{tianYiDir}}, {{yanNianDir}}, or {{fuWeiDir}} wall, ask what could move there instead. Storage out of the good corners, storage into the cautious ones, doors closed on all of it. That single rotation is most of what your storage asks of you, {{firstName}}, and it costs nothing but an afternoon.
