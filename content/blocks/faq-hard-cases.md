# The hard cases, {{firstName}}

Every home eventually asks a question this book's clean rules do not answer on their own. These are the five I am asked most, with the honest answers. None of them ends in "your home is wrong." Homes are never wrong. They are just specific.

## "My partner is in the opposite group"

This is the most common hard case, and the arithmetic behind it is blunt: for two people in opposite groups, the four directions that support one are exactly the four the other handles with care. There is no wall that flatters you both, so stop looking for it and share the home the way practitioners have always advised.

- **Split by function, not by fairness.** The tradition resolves a shared placement by asking *whose function it serves*. The bed mainly guards the sleep of whoever sleeps worst: their Tian Yi gets the headboard. The desk serves whoever works at it: their directions govern the chair.
- **Give each person their own seats.** Your reading chair faces your directions; your partner's chair faces theirs. Personal spaces follow the person.
- **For genuinely shared seats,** the tradition traditionally defers to the head of household, which in practice means: decide once, together, whose chart the dining orientation follows, and stop relitigating it at dinner.

One clear move: tonight, agree whose sleep the bed is protecting this season, and point the headboard at that person's Tian Yi. The Couple Compatibility Compass, if you want it, reads both charts together and maps every overlap.

## "I live in a studio"

One room does everything, so the eight-wedge map can feel like a joke. It is not; it just works at furniture scale instead of room scale.

- **Read the studio from its centre once,** then place *functions*, not rooms: the bed's headboard claims the best favourable wall, the desk chair takes a favourable facing, and storage absorbs the cautious wedges.
- **Let zones overlap without guilt.** A cautious wedge with a wardrobe and a laundry basket in it is a solved wedge, even if it is two steps from the bed.
- **Spend your effort on the two body positions** you hold longest, sleeping and working. In a studio those two choices are most of the game.

One clear move: give the headboard the best favourable wall the room offers, and hand the worst wedge the wardrobe.

## "The bed truly cannot move"

Radiator, window, door swing, sloped ceiling: some rooms offer exactly one bed position, and this book refuses to pretend otherwise.

- **Check which direction the fixed position actually is.** Half the time the immovable bed already points somewhere favourable, and the worry dissolves with one compass reading.
- **If it points at a cautious wall,** apply the softeners from that direction's chapter: a solid headboard, weight and fabric behind the head, one calm object beside it.
- **Then win the seats.** The desk chair and the evening chair almost always turn. Give your alert hours the favourable facings your sleeping hours cannot have.

One clear move: soften the bed once, properly, and transfer your ambition to the chair.

## "I rent"

No drilling, no repainting, and a landlord between you and every big idea. Good news: almost nothing in this book needs a drill.

- **Everything that matters here moves with you**: which wall the headboard leans on, which way the chairs face, what lives in which corner. That is the whole toolkit, and it is deposit-safe.
- **Use freestanding weight** where an owner would build: a leaned headboard, a bookcase as a room divider, a folding screen to break a sight-line.
- **Re-run the twenty-minute compass reading at every move.** Your directions never change; the walls do. The Room Map page is designed to be redone in pencil.

One clear move: treat your furniture placement, not the flat itself, as your feng shui. Renters often end up better at this system than owners, because they practise it fresh every lease.

## "My flat is open-plan"

No walls between kitchen, living, and dining: where does one sector end and the next begin?

- **The wedges do not need walls.** Stand at the centre, draw the eight directions, and let them fall across the open space. A sector can hold half the sofa. That is fine.
- **Anchor each zone with its furniture.** The dining table defines the dining zone wherever it stands; orient the seats, not imaginary rooms.
- **Use rugs, shelves, and lamps as soft walls** if you want the zones legible: a rug under the conversation corner, a low shelf behind the sofa. The tradition cares where your body settles and which way it faces, and open plans leave you freer to choose that than corridors ever did.

One clear move: place the seat you use most inside a favourable wedge, facing a favourable direction, and let the plan stay open around it.

## "I was born in January, and calculators disagree about me"

They disagree about the calendar, not about you. Your Kua comes from the *Chinese* year of your birth, and there are two conventions for where that year begins. This report uses the **Chinese New Year** date, which moves between late January and mid February. Classical compass lineages often use the solar term **Li Chun**, fixed around 4 February. Most years the two agree; some years they differ by days, and a birthday inside that gap gets a different year, so a different Kua, depending on the convention.

- **The arithmetic is never the problem.** When two calculators disagree about a January or early-February birth, the year boundary is almost always the reason.
- **A worked example:** someone born on 28 January 1990 belongs, for Kua purposes, to 1989 under both conventions, because both boundaries fall after that date in 1990.
- **If your birthday sits in the gap** between 4 February and that year's Chinese New Year, run the free calculator once with your real birth date and once as if born the year before, and read both profiles. The honest tiebreak is the same seven-day test this book uses for everything else: give the disputed bed or seat a week under each reading and keep the one your own mornings vote for.

One clear move: check the year boundary before doubting the reading. Boundary first, arithmetic second.

## "I live in the southern hemisphere"

A fair question with a genuinely split answer, and this book will not pretend otherwise.

- **The flip school** points out that the classical map encodes northern skies: south means the midday sun, north means winter cold. Below the equator the midday sun is in the north, so this school swaps north and south to follow the real sun. A minority of southern practitioners work this way, seriously and consistently.
- **The no-flip school** answers that compass feng shui is built on the earth's **magnetic field**, not the sun's path. A compass finds the same poles in Buenos Aires as in Beijing, so the system is one system worldwide. Notably, some manuals written *for* the southern hemisphere take exactly this position.
- **This report follows the no-flip convention:** your directions are read from the magnetic compass, unflipped, worldwide. If you prefer the flip school, apply it consistently to everything. What does not work is mixing the two conventions in one home.

One clear move: pick one convention, write it on your Room Map, and never mix.

## "Another calculator gave me a different Kua"

Two honest causes, in the order to check them.

- **The year boundary first.** If you were born in January or early February, see the January case above; this explains nearly all disagreements.
- **Formula variants second.** Lineages teach several arithmetic routes to the same eight profiles: one subtracts from ten, another from eleven on a different base; births from 2000 onward shift the digits again. Different-looking arithmetic that lands in the same classical Eight Mansions table is a variant, not an error.

One clear move: when in doubt, ignore the number and compare the *directions*. Two correct calculators may narrate the arithmetic differently, but your four supportive and four cautious directions should come out the same.
