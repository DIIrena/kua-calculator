# Your year, in method

Everything else in this report is fixed. Your Kua number, your group, your four good directions and your four cautious ones - those belong to you and do not change from one year to the next. This chapter is about a second layer that sits on top of all of that and moves on its own schedule, and it teaches you the method, so that every year of your life you know how to read the new arrangement when it arrives.

## The annual layer

The tradition reads each solar year as having its own arrangement: nine visiting influences, one per sector of any home, that swap positions when the year turns. The year begins, for this purpose, not on 1 January but at the solar term **Li Chun, around 4 February**, and practitioner almanacs publish the new arrangement each year at that boundary.

So you are always holding two maps at once. The first is yours and permanent: the directions tied to your Kua. The second is the year's, and it turns. Neither cancels the other. The skill, which this chapter hands you, is reading them together.

## The nine visitors, in household words

Each of the nine annual influences carries a classical name and a long file of associations. For daily use, practitioners compress them into plain life-domain labels, and the compressed version is the one worth memorising:

- **1** - connection and flow: movement, travel, new people
- **2** - common illness: the everyday-health caution
- **3** - conflict: friction, disputes, paperwork that bites
- **4** - study and creative work: the scholar's visitor
- **5** - chaos: the year's most serious caution, handled by stillness
- **6** - authority and the family economy
- **7** - loss and expenses: guard what leaks
- **8** - prosperity: the year's steadiest financial friend
- **9** - celebration and luck: recognition, milestones, good news

Which sector each visitor occupies in the current year is the year's actual chart, and that lives in the **Feng Shui Planner** for the year at hand. What never changes is what the numbers mean, and that is what you now hold.

## The rule that saves you from overwhelm

Here is the question everyone asks once they meet the annual layer: do I have to remedy every difficult visitor, every year, in every corner? The tradition's own answer is no, and the reason is a rule worth keeping for life:

**Stars work hardest where the home is most active.** The entrance, the open front rooms, the zones your household actually crosses all day: these amplify their visiting star, for good or ill. A storage room mutes its visitor almost completely. An afflicted star in a sealed box room is a note in the calendar; the same star at the front door is the year's main assignment.

So each year you check two or three placements, not nine. Where did the difficult visitors land, and does the household actually live there? If not, note it and move on. If yes, that spot gets the year's gentleness: calm use, no renovation, nothing new launched from that corner.

## Following the good visitors

The same rule has a bright side the beginner guides rarely teach: the auspicious visitors can be **followed**, not just noted. Practitioners read the annual chart as an opportunity map and deliberately route life through it.

- The sector hosting the year's **9** is the year's celebration corner: favour it for recognition, milestones, anything you want seen.
- The year's **8** marks steady accumulation: a fine host for the desk where the finances are managed.
- The year's **1** favours movement and new connections; **4** favours study and creative work; **6** favours discipline and matters of authority.

The claim is traditional. The practical effect is real either way: following the visitors forces you to use different parts of your home in different years, which is its own quiet corrective against rooms going stale.

## Where the year meets your map

Now lay the moving map over your fixed one, because the two speak loudest where they overlap.

- **Where a difficult visitor lands on one of your own cautious directions** - your {{huoHaiDir}}, {{wuGuiDir}}, {{liuShaDir}}, or {{jueMingDir}} - the tradition reads a doubly quiet corner for that year. Let it stay quiet. Not the place for the year's disruption, renovation, or the start of something demanding.
- **Where a supportive visitor meets one of your good directions** - your Sheng Qi ({{shengQiDir}}) or your Tian Yi ({{tianYiDir}}) - the tradition reads a corner worth deliberately using that year: the year's best seat, in your own personal grammar.

As a reader in the {{kuaGroup}} group, your fixed directions are the constant you measure each year against. You are not chasing the year; you are checking where it reinforces what you already know and where it asks for a lighter touch.

## The three still points

One more family of annual positions deserves its place in your method, because sooner or later a renovation, a landscaping project, or a major purchase will raise it. The tradition names three positions each year that share a single instruction: **do not disturb**.

- **Tai Sui, the Grand Duke,** occupies a narrow sector matching the year's zodiac animal. No major renovation, ground-breaking, or demolition there during its year; tradition adds that in confrontations one avoids sitting with the back directly to it. The old image is court etiquette: you do not dig at the feet of the year's presiding officer.
- **Sui Po, the Year Breaker,** is always the sector directly opposite Tai Sui, with the same instruction.
- **San Sha, the Three Killings,** is broader: a full quarter of the compass. Its rule has a memorable shape: **you may face it, but you must not have it at your back, and you do not renovate in it.**

Two practical softeners. First, exposure scales with activity, the same rule as before: a home that merely *contains* an afflicted sector feels it far less than one whose front door faces it. Second, there is a classical workaround for work that truly cannot wait: **start the project before the year turns at Li Chun.** A renovation already underway when the new positions arrive is not a fresh disturbance; a January start under the outgoing year's positions is the traditional escape.

Where these three sit in the current year, alongside all nine visitors, is exactly what the year's Planner maps sector by sector.

## What to do each February

The method, then, as a yearly habit that takes one evening:

1. When the year turns at Li Chun, get the year's chart (the Planner carries it, sector by sector, with the months).
2. Find the year's **5** and **2**, and check whether they landed anywhere your household actually lives. If yes, that corner gets the year's gentleness.
3. Find the year's **8** and **9**, and decide which one gets deliberately used this year.
4. Check the three still points against any building plans, and start before Li Chun if a project must touch them.
5. Lay it all over your fixed map on the Room Map page, in pencil, as always.
