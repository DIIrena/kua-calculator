# Your desk, {{firstName}}

Of all the placements in this reading, the desk is the one you can usually change today. The bed needs a wall and a clear path. The front door is fixed. But a chair turns. That is why the working-direction move is often the most useful adjustment you can make for someone whose work is the thing they push forward.

## The one rule that makes this simple

The system reads where your **body** faces while you are seated, not which way the desk points. So you turn the *chair*, not the desk. The desk can sit against a wall, float in the room, or stand at any angle the layout forces on it. None of that changes the reading. What the tradition reads is the direction your body opens toward while you work. Settle the chair first, then put the desk wherever it fits around it.

## Two directions for two kinds of work

You have two favourable directions worth reaching for at the work seat, and which one you choose depends on the kind of work in front of you.

- **Face {{shengQiDir}} for forward-pushing work.** This is your generating direction. The tradition associates it with drive, momentum, and ambitious effort - a launch, a hard piece of writing, a stretch where you want to push something out into the world. When the work is about *doing*, turn the chair so your body faces {{shengQiDir}}.
- **Face {{fuWeiDir}} for steady, deep-focus work.** This is your anchor direction. The tradition gives it to concentration, study, and quiet sustained attention. When the work needs you settled rather than charged - long careful reading, detailed work, anything that rewards patience - face {{fuWeiDir}} instead.

Both are correct placements. The question is simply which kind of day, or which kind of season, you are working through. Many people set the chair to {{fuWeiDir}} as the default and swap to {{shengQiDir}} when a push is on.

## What to avoid facing at the main seat

Where the room gives you a real choice, the tradition would not have your body face one of your cautious directions at the seat you use most. Two are worth naming here:

- **{{jueMingDir}}**, your most cautious direction.
- **{{wuGuiDir}}**, the next one the tradition would move away from.

If the layout forces it, that is fine - most rooms only allow so many arrangements, and a desk against the available wall beats no desk at all. But when you can choose, point the body toward {{shengQiDir}} or {{fuWeiDir}} and let it face away from {{jueMingDir}} and {{wuGuiDir}}. This is a fine adjustment, made only after you have settled the larger question of where you sit relative to the door.

## See the door, but not in its line

Independent of direction, the seat itself wants the command position. Two parts:

1. **You can see the door without turning.** Sitting with your back to the door is read across the tradition as a low-grade alert state - you can never quite settle, because something can enter behind you. Arrange the chair so the door is in view.
2. **You are not directly in line with the door.** Sitting square in front of an open door puts you in the rush of qi entering the room. The working compromise is the angle: place the seat so you see the door diagonally, off to one side, rather than facing straight down its path.

If the room cannot give you both the right direction and a view of the door, take the view of the door first. The command position is the rough adjustment; the favourable direction is the fine one tuned on top of it.

## A move to try

Turn your chair so your body faces {{fuWeiDir}} for your ordinary focused work, check that you can see the door from the side without sitting in its line, and reserve the swing to {{shengQiDir}} for the seasons when you are pushing something forward. Better than you had is the point, not perfection.

## The desk clinic

Four faults account for most working seats, and each has a settled repair. **Back to a window:** no backing, plus glare and draft; a high-backed chair and a partly drawn blind rebuild the mountain, or reposition if the room allows. **A mirror behind or facing the desk:** the exposure read, doubled; move the mirror or the desk. **A beam or a harsh light directly overhead:** pressure on the seat; shift the task light forward and to the side, or slide the desk off the beam's line. **The open-plan desk in a thoroughfare:** screen one side, anchor the other with storage, and give a long-occupancy seat a constructed back - a partition, a storage run, a high chair-back - because a seat with no mountain wears its owner down quietly.

The positive template never changes: solid wall behind, door visible, light from the side, one healthy plant in reach. Everything in the clinic above is just a route back to it.
