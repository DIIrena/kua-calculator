# Your Sheng Qi direction is {{direction}}

Sheng Qi is the *generating* direction, the strongest of your four favourable directions, and for you, {{firstName}}, it points {{direction}}.

## What Sheng Qi means

The full name is *Sheng Qi* (生氣), which translates to "generating qi" or, more loosely, "the source of energy." It is the senior officer of the four good directions. If Tian Yi (your healing direction) is for recovery, Yan Nian (your relationship direction) is for love, and Fu Wei (your anchor direction) is for steadiness, Sheng Qi is for *action*. It is where you go when you have something to push forward.

Practitioners have built rules around it for centuries. The first one, the most-cited, is the rule of the headboard: a bed whose headboard points to your Sheng Qi direction is said to wake the sleeper energetic, ambitious, ready. The second rule is the rule of the working seat: a desk where your body faces your Sheng Qi direction supports the kind of work that requires drive. The third is the rule of the front door: a home whose entrance opens to the Sheng Qi direction of the head of household is, by tradition, an auspicious home.

For you, the direction this lives in is **{{direction}}**.

If your front door opens roughly to this direction, the system reads it as a strong configuration. If it does not, that is fine. Most people do not get to choose their front door. The headboard and the desk are the moves you actually control, and those alone do most of the work the tradition associates with Sheng Qi.

## What Sheng Qi is for

The tradition gives Sheng Qi a specific job: it is the direction of *forward motion*. Not relaxation. Not love. Not study. Motion.

Use it for:

- **The bed**, when you are in a period of life that needs energy. A startup launch. A new job. Recovery from a hard year that left you tired and you are ready to be done being tired. Pointing the headboard {{direction}} for a season has been a Classical recommendation for centuries.
- **The desk**, when the work is ambitious. Big projects. Books being written. Cases being argued. Deals being closed. Face {{direction}} at the desk and the system says you are running with the wind on your back. (The chair faces this direction; the desk can face any direction. What the system reads is where the body looks.)
- **The negotiating seat.** When you are sitting across from someone and the stakes are high, take the chair that lets you face {{direction}}. This is the oldest single negotiation hack in Compass School practice and it costs nothing.
- **The morning corner.** The place you stand for the first cup of coffee. The window you look out of for the first thirty seconds of the day. Orient that corner so your body opens {{direction}}.

The pattern is the same in each of these uses. Where you have an active, ambitious, decisive function, your body should face Sheng Qi. Where you have a passive, recovering, settling function, your body should face one of the other three favourable directions instead.

Sheng Qi is not the right direction for everything. A bed pointed to Sheng Qi is energising, but a person who needs deep sleep more than they need energy is often better off pointing the bed to Tian Yi, the healing direction, which is the next chapter. Many Classical practitioners default the bed to Tian Yi for *most* of life and switch to Sheng Qi only during seasons that need a push. Both are correct readings. The question is which season you are in.

## What Sheng Qi feels like

When you are aligned with it, the tradition says, you wake on the right side of the day. You feel the work pull on you instead of the other way around. The first hour is *yours*. Decisions come faster. Confidence comes back. The thing you have been putting off becomes the thing you finish on Tuesday.

When you are pointed away from it, particularly when your bed is pointed at one of the four cautious directions instead of any of the four good ones, the felt-quality is the reverse. The mornings get heavier. The first hour gets eaten by a low-grade reluctance you cannot name. The work feels like *paddling against a current that is moving in the wrong direction*. You are not lazy. The room is not on your side.

You may have already noticed this. The job you took where the desk faced one way and the chair faced another, and within three weeks you knew the office was wrong but you could not say why. The hotel room on a work trip where you slept better than you have slept at home in months, and you did not know why, but the bed happened to point {{direction}}. The Kua system is a way of *naming* what was going on in both rooms.

## The hard thing

Most people cannot point their bed to their Sheng Qi direction without moving furniture they have lived with for years. The closet is on the wrong wall. The window is on the wrong wall. The radiator is on the wrong wall. The bedroom is small and there is only one way the bed fits.

Two honest things about this.

First, partial credit is real. If you cannot point the headboard to {{direction}}, point it to any of your other three favourable directions before you point it to a cautious one. Tian Yi, Yan Nian, and Fu Wei are all reasonable bed positions; the four to avoid are the four covered later in this book. The bed in *any* good direction beats the bed in a bad direction. The difference between Sheng Qi and Tian Yi for the headboard is real, but it is a fine adjustment. The difference between any good direction and any bad direction is the rough adjustment, and the rough adjustment is the one that actually shows up in the morning.

Second, the bed is not the only lever. If the bed has to stay where it is because the room is small, *change the desk*. The desk is almost always rearrangeable. Point your chair so your body faces {{direction}} when you work, and you have applied Sheng Qi to the part of the day where you actually generate. Many practitioners would argue that the working-direction move is more consequential than the sleeping-direction move for someone whose work is the main thing they generate. The sleeping move is about waking; the working move is about *doing*. Different jobs, both worth doing, both reading from the same Sheng Qi mapping.

The honest framing here is that perfect Sheng Qi alignment is rare and not the point. *Better than you had before* is the point. The system is a decision tool, not a discipline.

## How to apply it this week

You have a few hours to give it. Here is the order to spend them in.

Start with the desk. Identify which wall is on the {{direction}} side of the room. Move your chair so your body faces that wall when you sit at the desk. If the desk physically blocks you from facing {{direction}}, rotate the desk by ninety degrees, even if it puts you in a slightly awkward spot relative to the door for now. The body-facing direction is what the system reads, not the desk's footprint.

Move next to the bed, if it is movable. If the headboard is currently against the {{direction}} wall, the bed is already in Sheng Qi position. (Lucky you. Skip this step.) If the headboard is against any of your other three favourable walls, leave it. If the headboard is against one of your four cautious walls, move it to the closest favourable wall, even if it is not Sheng Qi. The chapter on the bed in the bedroom-specific products covers this in more detail; for now, *any* good direction is better than the bad one you might currently be on.

Then the morning corner. Pick the spot you stand in for the first cup of the day. Notice which direction your body opens to. If it is not {{direction}} (and it almost never is, the first time someone looks), turn the chair, move the table, swap the corner. Take one week of mornings facing {{direction}} and see if you notice the difference. The tradition says you will. The science of attention says you might. Either way, the test takes seven days.
