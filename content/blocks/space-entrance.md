# Your entrance, {{firstName}}

The entrance is the most worked-on square metre in this whole reading, {{firstName}}, and for a simple reason. It is where the home draws its energy in. The tradition calls the front door the mouth of the house: the opening through which qi arrives, and the first thing the home says to anyone, including you, who crosses it. Get the first few steps right and you have done the single highest-leverage piece of work a feng shui pass offers.

This is also the place where your personal Kua meets the fixed bones of your home, so it is worth being clear about what you can move and what you cannot.

## The door as the mouth of the house

Think of the door not as a hinge and a lock but as an intake. Energy gathers at the approach, enters through the opening, and then needs somewhere to go. Everything this reading says about the entrance follows from that one picture. A clear approach lets qi collect before it arrives. A clean threshold lets it cross without snagging. An open first few steps let it spread into the home rather than stalling at the mat or bouncing straight back out.

The tradition treats the door as the most important fixture in the home precisely because every other room is downstream of it. Whatever arrives, arrives here first.

## Your door and your {{shengQiDir}}

Here is where your Kua enters. You are in the {{kuaGroup}} group, Kua {{kuaNumber}}, and your generating direction - your Sheng Qi - points {{shengQiDir}}.

The tradition holds a specific rule about the front door: a home whose entrance opens toward the {{shengQiDir}} of the head of household is read as a strong configuration. It is one of the three classical door-bed-stove placements, and where it lines up, the system treats the home as well-fed at the mouth.

<p class="pull-quote">A door that opens to your {{shengQiDir}} is read as a strong configuration - but it is the one thing most people cannot choose, and the tradition knows it.</p>

So hold this gently. Most people do not get to pick which way their front door faces. It was decided by the builder, the street, the plot, long before you arrived. If your door happens to open roughly toward {{shengQiDir}}, the tradition reads that as a favourable starting point. If it does not, nothing is lost. The door is the hardest thing in the home to change, and it is also far from the most important thing you control.

What the tradition weights far more heavily is what you do *inside*: where the bed sits, where you work, where the first few steps land. Those are the moves that carry most of what this reading is about, and those are almost entirely yours to arrange. Read the door as information, not as a verdict.

## A clear approach and a clean threshold

Before the door does anything, the approach to it has to let energy gather. The path to your entrance is part of the entrance.

- **Easy to find, clearly the front door.** From the street or the corridor it should be obvious which door is the main one. A reader who has to hunt for the entrance is standing in for the qi that has to hunt for it too.
- **Lit and intact.** A working light over the threshold, paint that is not peeling, hardware that is solid, a number or name that reads cleanly. The tradition treats a tired, dim, or damaged door as a tired welcome.
- **Unobstructed swing.** The door should open fully into open space, not onto a shoe pile, a coat heap, or a stored box behind it. A door that cannot open all the way is a mouth that cannot fully open.

None of this is mystical. A clear, lit, undamaged approach is what the tradition reads as a home ready to receive, and it is also simply what a calm arrival feels like.

## The first few steps in

What you meet in the first second inside the door sets the read for the whole home. The tradition gives the entry hall a specific job: let the arriving energy land in open space and settle before it spreads.

- **Land in openness.** The first three steps inside should meet room to breathe, not a wall at the nose or a clutter of belongings. If the door opens onto a tight corner, a lamp, a small console, or a piece of art at eye level gives the eye somewhere to rest and the energy somewhere to gather.
- **Avoid the straight-through.** A direct line of sight from the front door to an opposing window or back door is read as energy entering and leaving in the same breath. Where you have that line, the tradition places something at chest height between the two - a console, a tall plant, a screen - to slow the path and let qi circulate rather than rush through.
- **A welcoming first impression.** A long view into a tidy room, a soft light, one living plant, art at the visual horizon. The tradition treats the entry as the home's handshake. A wall of coats, a heap of shoes, or a direct view into a bathroom is read as a weak one.

## Connecting the entrance to your directions

The entrance also sits in a fixed compass sector of your home, and that sector carries its own bagua reading - the same sector for everyone. What is *not* the same for everyone is whether that sector is one of your supportive directions or one to handle with care.

So check it against your own set rather than assume. If the compass sector your entrance falls in is one of your four supportive directions - your {{shengQiDir}}, {{tianYiDir}}, {{yanNianDir}}, or {{fuWeiDir}} - the tradition reads the entry as doubly well-placed for you, and the calm, lit, open treatment above lets that quality through. If your entrance instead falls in one of your four cautious directions - {{huoHaiDir}}, {{wuGuiDir}}, {{liuShaDir}}, or {{jueMingDir}} - treat the space gently: keep it especially clean, well lit, and uncluttered, and lean on the inside placements you fully control. Either way, the practical work is the same. The check just tells you how much the tidiness is doing for you.

## A move to try tonight

The entrance is the rare high-leverage fix you can finish in fifteen minutes. Clear the threshold: move every shoe and bag out of the door's swing arc so it opens fully. Wipe the door, polish the handle, check that the light works. Then look at where the first three steps land and give that spot one calm anchor - a small lamp, a mat, a single plant, one piece of art at eye level.

You will not have changed which way your door faces, {{firstName}}, and you do not need to. You will have given the mouth of your home a clear, lit, open place to draw energy in, which is the part the tradition reads most closely and the part that was always yours to set.
