# Your helpful-people corner, {{firstName}}

The tradition keeps one corner of the home for the people who help you from the outside: mentors, allies, useful contacts, the person who knows the person, the colleague who vouches for you. The same corner is read as the one tied to travel and to movement beyond your own walls. To work with it, {{firstName}}, you first have to find it. The tradition maps this theme to the **Northwest** of a space, so the move is to stand with a compass, locate the Northwest of your home or of a single room, and read what is actually sitting there. The compass direction is the same for any space; what you do with the corner you find is the part this reading is about.

## What the tradition puts here

The Northwest carries the trigram *Qian* and the element **Metal**. In the eight-area map, this is the sector the tradition associates with *helpful people* and *travel*, the two read as one theme, because both are about reaching outward and being met halfway. The helpful-people side covers mentors, patrons, benefactors, and the loose web of contacts who open doors you cannot open alone. The travel side covers journeys, relocations, and movement that takes you outside the familiar.

Hold the language carefully. The tradition *associates* this corner with support and useful connection. It does not promise that tending it will deliver a mentor or a trip. The reading is a decision tool, not a forecast. What it gives you is a place to direct attention, and a set of long-standing rules for how to direct it.

*Qian* is also the trigram the tradition links to the head of the household and to authority in general. That is why it reads the Northwest as the seat of the figures who hold some standing in your life, and why the corner carries two labels that turn out to be one theme: mentors and benefactors on one side, travel on the other. Both are the same outward reach, toward people and places beyond your own walls, and both answer to this one corner.

The tradition's star vocabulary keeps a name for exactly this theme, too. The 6 White, sometimes called the noble-person star, is the one it associates with support arriving through people senior to you: the manager who argues your case, the elder who opens a door. You do not need the star system to work with this corner. It is simply worth knowing that the theme runs through more than one layer of the tradition, not just this map.

## Keeping it active and tidy

Metal corners reward order. The single most-cited instruction for the Northwest is the plainest one: keep it clear. The tradition treats clutter here as the thing that quietly works against the corner's theme, because a blocked corner is read as blocked support. If the Northwest of your home, or of a single room, has become the place where bags pile up, where the broken thing waits to be fixed, where the door cannot fully open, that is the first thing to address. Not because clearing it earns you anything, but because the tradition reads a tended corner and a neglected one differently, and this is the one you can actually control.

*Active* is the second word the tradition uses for this sector. A corner that gets touched, walked through, looked at, and used is read as a living one. A spare room in the Northwest that nobody enters for weeks is read as a sleeping corner. You do not need to build a shrine. You need to give the space a reason to be visited.

## What to place there

The tradition gives the Northwest a short, practical placement list. None of these is a charm. Each is a way of marking the corner's theme so the space reads as deliberate rather than forgotten.

- **Metal, because the corner's element is Metal.** Round metal objects, a metal bowl, a small clock, metal frames, coins kept in a dish. The tradition reads metal here as reinforcing the corner rather than fighting it.
- **Grey, white, and metallic tones**, the colours the tradition assigns to Metal. A cushion, a mat, a frame in those tones is a quiet way to honour the sector without rearranging the room.
- **Images of the people or places you associate with support.** Photographs of those who have helped you, or of a place you intend to travel to. The tradition treats the image as a reminder to the resident more than a signal to the room.
- **Something that suggests movement**, for the travel side: a globe, a map, an object brought back from somewhere. Light and openness matter here too; a corner you can see into reads as more active than one in shadow.

Avoid overloading it. A Metal corner crowded with heavy Earth objects (large ceramics, stone, dense pottery) is read by the element logic as Earth burying Metal, which works against the sector. One or two deliberate objects beat a shelf of them.

## Two symbols the tradition keeps for this corner

Most feng shui objects sold today float freely around the home, placed wherever a shop suggests. Two are genuinely native to the Northwest, and both are rarer and quieter than the usual shopfront pieces.

The first is the **Qilin**, the benevolent chimera of Chinese art, part deer, part dragon, part horse. The tradition assigns it to mentor luck: the arrival of a wise, well-placed ally. It is a far less commercial figure than a money frog, and the Northwest is its seat. You do not need one; if the figure happens to appeal to you, this is the corner it belongs in.

The second is the **six-rod metal wind chime**, and here the number is doing the work. Six is the number the tradition pairs with heaven and cooperation, and Metal is *Qian*'s own element, so a chime of exactly six metal rods matches this corner on both counts. Hang it where air actually reaches it now and then; a chime that never stirs is just another object on a hook.

## Fire at Heaven's Gate

The tradition's old name for the Northwest is Heaven's Gate, and its sharpest caution for this corner follows from the name. A stove sitting on the exact Northwest of the home is read as *fire at Heaven's Gate*: fire burning in the benefactor sector. Of all the room-and-sector overlaps in the classical texts, this is one of the most consistently flagged.

If your kitchen happens to sit there, nothing is wrong with your house and nobody expects you to move a kitchen. The working mitigation is modest. Keep the stove immaculate, wiped down and fully working, every burner functional. Then give it an earth buffer nearby: ceramic or stone within arm's reach of the hob, a stoneware jar, a stone trivet, a tiled surface. In the element cycle, fire produces earth and earth produces metal, so the tradition reads the earth pieces as stepping the fire's heat down toward the corner's own element instead of letting it burn straight at the metal.

Then place one Metal object you actually like in it. That is the whole move.
