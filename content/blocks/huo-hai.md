# Your Huo Hai direction is {{direction}}

Huo Hai is the gentlest of the four directions the tradition treats with caution. Its name, *Huo Hai* (<span lang="zh-Hant">禍害</span>), translates as **Mishap**, and for you, {{firstName}}, it points **{{direction}}**.

Before anything else: nothing about this quarter of your compass is dangerous. Four of your directions are read as supportive and four are handled with more care, and Huo Hai is the mildest of the careful four. What the next four chapters hand you is not a hazard map. It is a filing system.

## What this means

The tradition associates Huo Hai with low-grade friction: misplaced keys, a draught no one can trace, the kind of thing that does not spoil a day and is gone by the next one. It reads this as a direction that accumulates rather than strikes, and even the accumulation is mild. In practice that makes Huo Hai a **low-priority** placement, not a harmful one. The tradition would rather your bed and your serious work face supportive walls; this corner it hands, with a clear conscience, to everything that does not need a good direction at all.

Most homes already have a corner like this. Many households have been using it well for years without a name for what they were doing.

## Give it this

- **Storage.** Cupboards, closets, the seasonal coats, the boxes of decorations, the cables you keep meaning to sort.
- **The laundry zone.** Washing machine, drying rack, ironing board. The function happens, ends, and no one dwells there.
- **The hobby shelf.** The sewing machine you reach for one Sunday in three, the bin of yarn, the model paints. Loved, but only periodically needed.
- **The kept-but-rarely-touched.** The holiday dishes, the teapots that never pour. Objects that earn their place by being kept, not used.

## Keep off it

- **The bed, if a supportive wall is free.** The headboard wall sets the direction of the sleeping head, and you have four supportive walls to offer it first.
- **The main desk.** If the room only works one way, do not move the room: turn the chair so your body faces a favourable direction and let the desk sit where it sits.
- **The chair for hard conversations,** as a gentle preference rather than a rule to lose sleep over.

## When you cannot avoid it

Sometimes the flat forces one important function onto a less-than-ideal wall. If that is your situation, here is the honest ranking: Huo Hai is the **least costly** of the four cautious directions to land on. Not Wu Gui, not Liu Sha, not Jue Ming. This one. Give the bed a solid headboard, keep that side of the room tidy and closed rather than open and spilling, and then stop thinking about it. The tradition reads this wall as low priority, and low priority is a mild thing.

## Practitioner tip

The first time someone reads that a quarter of their compass is "to be careful of," they tense up and start eyeing the spare room. I ask them to walk to the corner and describe what is actually there. Nine times out of ten they list the laundry, the boxes, the rarely-opened cupboard, and I get to give them the good news: you have been doing this correctly for years. The tradition is naming what the household already chose, not demanding a renovation.

## A real home

Petra is Kua 2, so her Huo Hai points east. Her east-facing box room held exactly what you would guess: the drying rack, two suitcases, the winter duvet in a vacuum bag, and a filing crate. When she first ran her chart she assumed the "mishap" room needed fixing and priced up a conversion to a study. Her Room Map talked her out of it. The study went to her Fu Wei corner instead, the box room kept the suitcases, and the only change the east room got was a sturdier shelf and a door that now closes properly. Total spend: one shelf. The cautious direction was already doing the exact job the tradition would assign it.
