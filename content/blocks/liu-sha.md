# Your Liu Sha direction is {{direction}}

There is a project sitting in your email. You committed to it eight months ago in a meeting that ran long, and three weeks in you knew it was the wrong project, and you kept going anyway. The email thread is forty replies deep. None of the replies has moved the thing forward. Every time you open it you tell yourself you will reply on Sunday. Every Sunday you do not. You have not noticed, exactly, that the same thing has happened with a kitchen renovation and a friendship and a side business, all of them in slow motion, all of them quietly off the rails, none of them dramatic enough to *end*. They just keep going. That feeling, the feeling of a plan that has *quietly stopped working* and that nobody has been brave enough to name, is the felt-quality the tradition would call Liu Sha.

## What Liu Sha means

The full name is *Liu Sha*, which translates literally as "Six Killings." The name sounds frightening. The phenomenon is not. The tradition reads Liu Sha as the direction associated with **broken plans**, lost opportunities, mishaps that compound, drawn-out misunderstandings, low-grade legal trouble, and the kind of emotional volatility that does not announce itself as a crisis because it has been building for too long.

It is the third of your four cautious directions. Sharper than *Huo Hai* (the mildest, where small things go small ways wrong) and sharper than *Wu Gui* (the arguments and the misplaced keys), but not as severe as *Jue Ming*, which comes next. Liu Sha sits in the middle of that gradient. It is not catastrophe. It is the *steady erosion* that makes a household feel tired without anyone being able to name why.

For you, the direction this lives in is **{{direction}}**.

The classical reading is simple: do not anchor any primary function here. The honest reading is sharper. A corner that you turn into a pass-through, a corridor, or a closet often functions better than a corner you have asked to support a primary activity and that has been quietly refusing for years.

## The lived test

You probably already know this without needing a system to tell you.

Call up the memory of the spare room nobody actually uses. The room you told yourself would be the home office, then the guest room, then the place the exercise bike would live, then the place the exercise bike now lives unused under a small drift of laundry. You went into that room with three different plans. None of them stuck. The room is not broken. The room has, with quiet odds, been a Liu Sha sector of the home for someone in the household, and the body has been picking that up and refusing to settle in there, plan after plan.

Or call up the office layout that never quite worked. The desk you moved three times. The meeting that always felt off in that corner of the open-plan, and that nobody could fix by ordering different chairs. The seating arrangement at a family table that, every gathering, ended in someone leaving early. None of these are random. The body is doing its old work. Some directions hold; some directions release; some directions, for a given person, do the slow steady release that the tradition has had a name for since the Ming dynasty.

It is not bad luck. It is *placement*.

## The hard thing

Here is where Liu Sha goes wrong, and it goes wrong in two specific ways.

The first is the person who put the bed here without realising. The headboard sits against the wall on the Liu Sha side of the room and has done so for three years. They are not sleeping badly enough to seek help. They are sleeping badly enough that mornings are slightly heavier than they should be, the snooze button is hit twice, the first coffee does less work than it used to. They are also, in a way they cannot quite articulate, making slightly worse decisions than they used to. The promotion they did not push for. The friendship they let drift. The doctor's appointment they have rebooked four times. The tradition would call this *the bed pointing the body in the wrong direction for long enough that the body forgets there is a right direction*. The modern reader can call it the slow accumulation of small unsettling inputs during the eight hours the nervous system is most plastic.

The second is the person whose room simply does not allow anything else. The bedroom is small. The window is on one wall, the door is on another, the radiator is on a third, the closet door swings into the fourth. There is exactly one place the bed fits, and that place points the headboard at {{direction}}. The honest answer to this person is not "move the bed anyway." The honest answer is that you do what the room allows, and you apply the softening moves the tradition has worked out for exactly this case.

What the tradition would call a *softener* on the headboard wall is, in practice, three things. A solid headboard, the kind that has weight, not a frame with bars or a poster of slats. A heavy textile against the wall, a tapestry or a thick rug hung vertically, that *insulates* the head from the wall behind it. And one calm object on the bedside table that you actually look at: a small lamp with a warm bulb, a single photograph in a frame, the kind of thing that gives the eye somewhere to land in the moments before sleep. The point is not magic. The point is that the wall the body sleeps against gets to carry weight, fabric, and one quiet visual anchor, and the room stops asking the body to do all the steadying work on its own.

The bed pointed {{direction}} with those three softeners in place is not the same as the bed pointed at one of your four favourable directions. The tradition is honest about that. But it is also not the same as the bed pointed {{direction}} with nothing on the wall behind it, and the difference is real, and most people in cramped rooms can do this in an afternoon.

## The fork

What to put on the {{direction}} wall when the choice is yours.

**Hallways and corridors.** A {{direction}} hallway is doing the work {{direction}} is best at: carrying you through it without asking you to stay. The body passes through, the room releases the body, neither party is asked to settle. This is the highest and best use of a Liu Sha sector in any home where the floor plan allows it.

**The laundry, the utility room, the linen closet.** Rooms whose function is *containment* of things you only touch on a schedule. The system reads these uses as appropriate because the room is not being asked to support an anchored daily activity. The washing machine does its work. You enter, you leave, the room releases.

**The pass-through space.** The bit of room between the kitchen and the dining table. The landing at the top of the stairs that holds a single chair nobody actually sits in. The mud room. The bit of corridor where the dog's water bowl lives. The tradition would call all of these *correct uses*. They occupy the sector without asking it to perform.

**The closet, the deep storage, the seasonal-decoration cupboard.** The Christmas lights live here. The suitcase you use twice a year lives here. The single rollerblade from a hobby you tried in 2019 and never finished lives here. These are all appropriate Liu Sha tenants. They take up space, they ask nothing of the direction, they release the room from having to support a primary function.

**The room you only use as transit.** Some homes have one. The corner of the kitchen behind the table that nobody ever stands in for longer than a minute. The end of the hallway where the air gets slightly cooler. These are the spots the body has already been treating as Liu Sha. Naming it just lets you stop feeling guilty about not putting a desk there.

If the bed must point {{direction}}, the chapter does not pretend otherwise. The softeners on the headboard wall are the move. The other lever, almost always, is the desk. *Move the desk* so that your body faces one of your four favourable directions when you work, and you have applied the rough adjustment to the part of the day where you are most awake and most generating. The bed is the long slow input. The desk is the short sharp input. If the long slow input cannot move, the short sharp input must.
