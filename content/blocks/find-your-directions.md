# Find your eight directions, {{firstName}}

You now know your directions by name. This chapter turns the names into walls.

Everything after this page assumes you can stand in your own home and point at your {{shengQiDir}} wall without guessing. That takes a phone, a piece of paper, and about twenty minutes. Do it once and it is done for as long as you live there.

## What you need

- **A compass.** The compass app on your phone is enough. A physical compass is even better, because phones can be nudged by wiring and appliances. Take your reading a step away from the fridge, the TV, and anything with a motor in it, and keep the compass away from your phone, your belt buckle, and the building's metal fittings while you read.

When a reading matters, use the field protocol practitioners use. Take **three readings along the same wall**: far left, far right, and centre, standing the same distance out from the wall each time. The reason is honest physics: reinforced concrete, parked cars, and buried services all bend a magnetic bearing locally, and three points reveal what one point hides. If the three agree, any of them serves. **If they disagree, use the centre reading.**
- **Paper and a pencil.** A rough sketch, not a blueprint. Boxes for rooms.
- **Twenty quiet minutes.** Walk it alone the first time. It goes faster and you notice more.

One small note before you start. A phone compass points to magnetic north, and formal surveying corrects that reading slightly. You do not need to. Each of the eight sectors is forty-five degrees wide, which is a generous target. Room-level accuracy is all this work asks for.

## Step 1: Sketch your floor plan

Draw the outline of your home as seen from above, one box per room, roughly in proportion. Label the rooms. Mark the front door with a small notch. This does not need to be pretty. It needs to be yours.

## Step 2: Stand at the centre

Walk to roughly the middle of your home and stand there. If your home is an L-shape or a long corridor, the middle of the part you live in most is fine. A rough centre is all the tradition asks; nobody is measuring you.

## Step 3: Find north

Open the compass and turn your body until it reads north, which is zero degrees. Look at what you are facing: a wall, a window, the hallway, the neighbour's kitchen. Now mark a small **N** on the edge of your sketch on that side. This is the moment the whole reading hinges on, and it takes ten seconds.

## Step 4: Draw the eight directions

From the centre of your sketch, draw a line toward your N mark, then divide the page around it like a clock with eight hours: north, northeast, east, southeast, south, southwest, west, northwest, working clockwise. Each direction owns a wedge of your home, forty-five degrees wide.

If you prefer degrees, here is the full dial in one line: N is 0, NE is 45, E is 90, SE is 135, S is 180, SW is 225, W is 270, NW is 315, and each sector runs from about 22 degrees before its number to 22 degrees after.

## Step 5: Read one room closely

The whole-home view tells you which rooms sit where. For furniture moves you also want the room-level view, and it rests on a rule worth knowing by name: **each room repeats the whole-house grid in miniature**. North in the house is north in every room of it, and so is every other direction. This is the rule that makes the system renter-proof: even when you control a single room, all eight of your directions exist inside it at full strength. Stand in the middle of your bedroom, find north again, and name the four walls. Now the sentences in the chapters ahead become physical. "The headboard on your {{tianYiDir}} wall" stops being an idea and becomes *that wall, the one with the picture on it*.

Do the same in the room where you work. Those two rooms, the bedroom and the workroom, carry most of what this book will ask you to do.

Two habits make this map more useful. First, for seats and beds, record the direction the body faces or the head points, not the room's name, because that is what the system reads. Second, write in pencil. Homes change, furniture moves, and a map you can update is a map you will keep using.

## If the reading surprises you

It often does. The room you call "the back bedroom" turns out to sit northeast. The desk you thought faced east faces southeast. This is normal, and it is good news: it means you measured instead of assumed. I have walked through many homes with the owner holding the compass, and the small surprise at this step is usually the moment the book stops being theory for them.

<p class="one-thing"><strong>If you only do one thing:</strong> stand in the middle of your bedroom tonight, find north on your phone, and name the wall your headboard sits against. Every chapter after this one gets easier once you have done it.</p>
