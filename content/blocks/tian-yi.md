# Your Tian Yi direction is {{direction}}

Tian Yi is the direction the tradition turns to for rest. Of your four supportive directions it is the one most often given to the bed in an ordinary season of life, and for you, {{firstName}}, it points **{{direction}}**.

The pinyin is *Tian Yi* (<span lang="zh-Hant">天醫</span>), which translates as **Heavenly Doctor**. A heavenly doctor is not a cure you swallow. It is the corner of the house the tradition treats as the place a body is allowed to mend.

## What this means

Here is the line worth sitting with before you decide anything: in Classical practice, for most people in most ordinary seasons, **the headboard goes to Tian Yi, not Sheng Qi**. This surprises people. Sheng Qi is the famous one, the direction the brochures lead with. But the working tradition, the readings given for grandmothers and neighbours over centuries, defaults the bed here.

The reasoning is plain. Most life is not a launch. Most weeks are the slow accumulation of a body that does laundry, goes to work, has feelings about its parents, catches a cold in October. The tradition reads the Sheng Qi bed as suited to a stretch of active pushing, and the Tian Yi bed as suited to the long ordinary stretches that make up most of a life. Neither ranks above the other. They belong to different **seasons**.

## Do this

- **Put the headboard on the {{direction}} wall** for ordinary seasons, so the top of your head points {{direction}} while you sleep. This is the single placement the tradition would set first in most homes.
- **Give the head of the household the {{direction}}-facing dining seat.** The tradition has seated elders facing Tian Yi at family meals for generations; the reading is steadiness, not any promise about digestion.
- **Keep the objects of recovery on the {{direction}} side.** The first-aid box, the chamomile tea, the shelf the vitamins live on, the small unopened jar of capers you bought for one recipe in 2019 and have never thrown out. A convalescent room, if your home has one, follows the same logic.

## Avoid this

- **Do not chase Sheng Qi for the bed out of ambition alone.** An energising bed in a tired season works against the very rest you need. The push season is the exception; this direction is the rule.
- **Do not move the bed weekly.** Choose the wall, give it a full week with the seven-day method, and let the room settle. A bed that keeps migrating tells you nothing.
- **Do not sacrifice the practical for the directional.** If the {{direction}} wall puts the bed under a draughty window or hard against the door swing, the tradition loses to the draught. Take the best favourable wall the room actually offers.

## Practitioner tip

When I read a bedroom, the first question is not where Tian Yi is. It is "how have you been sleeping". Someone who wakes groggy in a room that already points to a favourable wall does not need a compass fix; they need the streetlight blocked and the phone out of the room. The direction work sits on top of the ordinary sleep hygiene, never instead of it. When both point the same way, the room is doing everything a room can do.

## A real home

Marco and his partner bought their first flat last spring. Marco is Kua 6, so his Tian Yi points northeast. The bedroom offered two workable walls: the northeast wall, bare, and the west wall, where the previous owner had left picture hooks and where the bed "obviously" went. They tried the obvious wall for a month while boxes emptied. Then, out of curiosity more than conviction, they moved the headboard to the northeast wall and gave it a week. What Marco noticed was not mystical: the new position happened to turn his sleeping face away from the gap in the curtains, and the room read calmer the moment the bed stopped facing the door straight on. The tradition would say the headboard found Tian Yi. A skeptic would say the bed found the quieter corner. Both descriptions point to the same wall, which is rather the point of the system.

## If the bed cannot move

Sometimes the room has one possible bed position, and it is not this one. Then the tradition's second-best move is the seat: turn the desk chair, or the reading chair, so your body faces **{{direction}}** for a restful part of the day. A chair turns far more easily than a bed. And if another of your favourable walls can take the headboard, that beats a cautious wall every time; the fine difference between two good walls matters far less than the rough difference between good and cautious.
