## What Tian Yi means

The pinyin is *Tian Yi*, which translates literally as **Heavenly Doctor**. It is the second of your four favourable directions in the classical ranking, and in everyday Compass School practice it is often the most useful one of the four.

The tradition gives it a specific job. Where Sheng Qi is for generating, and Yan Nian is for loving, and Fu Wei is for steady focused thought, Tian Yi is for *healing*. The word does not only mean medical recovery, though it does mean that. It also means the quieter recovery a body does between Tuesday and Wednesday, the slow steady mending that happens during deep sleep, the way a long bath at the end of a hard day takes a particular kind of tiredness out of your shoulders and a different kind out of your jaw.

Practitioners read Tian Yi as the direction of the restorative function. The body knows what to do with rest. Tian Yi is the direction you point the body in while it does it.

## The most common bedroom direction in Classical practice

Here is the line that should sit on your fridge for a week before you decide anything about it.

In Classical Compass School practice, for *most* people in *most* ordinary seasons of life, the headboard goes to **Tian Yi**, not Sheng Qi.

This is the move that surprises people the first time they hear it. Sheng Qi is more famous. Sheng Qi is the senior officer. Sheng Qi is the direction the brochures lead with, because growth and ambition and "generating qi" sound like the things you would want from a feng shui consultation. But the working practitioners, the ones who have been arranging beds for forty years for their grandmothers and their neighbours and their grandmothers' neighbours, default the bed to Tian Yi.

The reason is simple, and it is the reason every honest paragraph in this book comes back to. Most life is not a launch. Most months are not a startup. Most weeks are the slow accumulation of a body that does laundry and goes to work and has feelings about its parents and gets a cold in October and needs the same bed, in the same direction, to mend it. The bed in Sheng Qi position is *brilliant* for the six months you are launching the thing or pushing yourself out of a tired patch. The bed in Tian Yi position is the bed that holds up across the other twenty years.

The system does not say one is better than the other. It says they are for different seasons. The honest framing is that **Tian Yi is the default**, and Sheng Qi is the substitution you make for a known reason during a known stretch of time.

## What Tian Yi is for

Use it for:

- **The bed**, in any season of life that is not actively a push. Long ordinary months. Recovery from a winter that broke you a little. The year you decided to stop trying so hard and just live for a while. Point your headboard {{direction}} and the system reads the bed as supporting the conditions for *restorative* sleep.
- **The dining seat**, especially the head-of-household chair. The tradition has been seating elders facing Tian Yi at family meals for centuries. The reading is that the body that eats facing its healing direction supports the conditions for digestion, for the slow conversion of food into a person who is well.
- **The chair you go to when you are unwell.** The corner of the sofa during a flu. The reading nook during a hard week. The bath, if the bath could be said to point in a direction (it cannot, but the wall it is against can, and you can lie with your head toward {{direction}} and the tradition reads that as a Tian Yi position).
- **The convalescent room**, if there is one. The room where someone is recovering from a surgery, or a grief, or a long stretch of overwork. Point the bed in that room {{direction}}.
- **The cabinet for wellness things.** The shelf the vitamins live on, the first-aid box, the chamomile tea, the small unopened glass jar of capers you bought for one recipe in 2019 and have never thrown out. The tradition reads the wall on the {{direction}} side of a room as the appropriate wall for the objects of recovery.

The pattern in each of these uses is the same. Where the body's job is to *mend*, the body should face Tian Yi.

## The lived test

You probably know this one already, even if you have never had words for it.

Call up the bedroom you slept best in. Not the bedroom you remember most fondly; the one where you actually slept. The one where you woke and your body felt like it had been *put back together* in the night. Almost certainly the headboard in that room pointed to a favourable Kua direction, and very often, in the way people seem to do this by accident, that direction was Tian Yi.

Or the long convalescence. The flu that took eleven days, the surgery that took six weeks, the bereavement that took the autumn. The chair you sat in for those weeks, the corner of the house you migrated to almost without thinking, the cushion you dragged from one room to another. Where in the home was it? What direction did your body face when you sat down there? People do this on instinct, and on instinct they tend toward the healing corner.

The tradition's claim is that the body knows. It has always known. The Kua system is the part of the practice that lets you *name* what the body already does.

The cheaper version of the test runs in the next two weeks. Try a fortnight sleeping with your headboard pointed {{direction}}. Compare it to whatever the previous fortnight was. The difference is not always dramatic. Sometimes it is the absence of a low-grade tiredness you had stopped noticing, like the absence of a hum from a fridge that has finally been replaced. Sometimes it is the morning your skin looks like skin again. Sometimes you wake up and you do not feel forty years old at thirty, for the first time in months, and you cannot decide whether it is the bed or the coffee or the fact that you stopped reading the news.

The test takes fourteen days. It costs nothing except moving a piece of furniture.

## The hard thing

Here is the place where the book stops flattering you.

A lot of people pick Sheng Qi for their bed because it sounds more glamorous, and then they wonder, two years later, why their sleep is shallow and why the mornings feel hectic and why they wake up already a little behind. They are not doing anything wrong. They have just chosen a direction the tradition would have given a *different* person, in a *different* season, and they have stayed on that direction for a season longer than the body was built for.

The Sheng Qi bed is for the year you are pushing. It is not for the year you are recovering. It is not for the year you are simply living, doing the laundry, raising the child, finishing the slow piece of work that does not need a launch. If your bed is in Sheng Qi position and you have been *tired* in a way that does not match the amount of sleep you appear to be getting, look at the headboard. Look at where it points. Ask yourself, honestly, whether the body in that bed needs a generator or a doctor right now.

The other hard thing is geometry. Sometimes the bed cannot move. The wall is the wrong wall, the radiator is in the wrong place, the window is too low, the room is too small, the door swings the wrong way, and the bed in this room has exactly one possible orientation and you have been sleeping in it for nine years. The tradition has thought about this. There is a second-best move, and it is the one most working practitioners reach for when the bed is fixed.

**The desk becomes the lever.**

If the bed cannot point to Tian Yi, point your chair at it instead. A desk where your body faces {{direction}} when you sit at it gives you a Tian Yi position during the part of the day where you are doing the work that is hardest on you. The reading the tradition makes is that a body that *recovers while it works* is the next best thing to a body that recovers while it sleeps. It is not the same. The bed move is bigger. But the desk move is not nothing, and the desk almost always moves more easily than the bed.

The honest framing is that perfect Tian Yi alignment is rare. *Better than you had before* is the point. You are looking for the room to stop fighting you, not for the room to do all the work.

Move the bed if you can. If the bed is fixed and cannot move, leave it. Do not torture the room. Go to the desk instead and rotate the chair so that when you sit at it your body opens to {{direction}}. The desk's footprint does not matter; what the system reads is *where your body looks*. Move the lamp if you have to. Add a cushion to the chair so your hips sit at the right height. Take the cat off the keyboard. (The cat has been there for some time and is not going to move on its own.)

Then the dining table. Find the seat that lets you face {{direction}} when you eat. Take it for a week and see whether dinner feels different. If it does, take it permanently. If you live alone, this is the easiest single Compass School move there is, because no one is going to challenge you for the chair.

Then the wellness shelf, if you have one. The bathroom cabinet, the kitchen drawer with the medicines, the corner of the bedside table that holds your vitamins. Move it to the {{direction}} wall of whichever room it lives in. This is the smallest, fussiest move on the list and the one most people skip. Do it anyway. The tradition takes the objects of recovery seriously, and so should you.
