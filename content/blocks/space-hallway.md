# Your hallways, {{firstName}}

A hallway is not a room you live in. It is the space between rooms, the connective tissue the rest of the home hangs on. Nobody sits in a corridor for an evening. But you pass through it many times a day, and the tradition treats that passage as worth getting right, because a hallway is the path qi takes to reach everywhere else.

## A pass-through space, by design

Because a corridor is movement rather than rest, the tradition is relaxed about which compass sector it occupies. The rooms where you spend hours, the ones where the bed sits, where you work, where you eat, are the ones it wants on your supportive directions, the {{kuaGroup}} group set of {{shengQiDir}}, {{tianYiDir}}, {{yanNianDir}}, and {{fuWeiDir}}. A hallway carries no such weight. You are never still in it long enough for its orientation to register the way a bedroom does.

That makes a corridor one of the few places the tradition is happy to hand to a direction it would otherwise treat with care. If a hallway runs along {{liuShaDir}}, or sits in any of your four cautious sectors of {{huoHaiDir}}, {{wuGuiDir}}, {{liuShaDir}}, and {{jueMingDir}}, that is not a problem to solve. A pass-through space is exactly where a cautious direction earns its keep. You move through it and move on. So when you are mapping your home and find a corridor on one of your harder directions, read it as a small relief rather than a fault. It means a room you care about more is not sitting there instead.

## Keep the path clear

The single rule the tradition holds firmest for corridors is the simplest. Keep them clear.

A hallway is qi's route through the home. When it is pinched by a shelf of overflow clutter, a row of boxes waiting to go upstairs, or a vacuum cleaner that never made it back to the cupboard, movement narrows, and the tradition reads narrowed movement as a home that does not breathe freely. The fix is the cheapest in the whole reading. Anything that does not need to live in the hallway, move out of it.

- A corridor is for passing through, not for storing.
- Shoes, coats, and bags belong behind a door, not stacked along the wall of the route.
- If something has lived in the hallway for months because there is nowhere else for it, that is a sign a cupboard somewhere else is the real problem to solve.

## Light in a dark corridor

Corridors are the rooms most often left dim. They have no window of their own as a rule, they borrow light from the doors that open off them, and a single burnt-out bulb in a hallway can go unnoticed for weeks because nobody lingers to read by it.

The tradition treats a dark passage as a stalled one. Qi follows light, and a corridor with no light of its own becomes a dead stretch between the bright rooms it serves. The remedy is ordinary. Put working light in, keep every bulb along the run at the same warm temperature so the corridor reads as one continuous space rather than a string of bright and dark patches, and replace bulbs the day they go rather than the month after. A well-lit hallway is the easiest correction in any home and the most consistently overlooked.

## A long straight run

A short hallway needs only light and clear floor. A long straight one asks for a little more.

When a corridor runs four or five metres or more in a dead straight line with nothing to catch the eye, the tradition reads it as a small wind tunnel. Qi is meant to wander gently through a home, settling as it goes. A long unbroken run lets it pick up speed and rush instead of drift, and a corridor that rushes feels subtly uncomfortable to walk even when you cannot name why.

The cure is not to block the path. It is to slow it. You want the eye to pause every few steps, because where the eye slows, the tradition reads qi as slowing too.

- **A runner on the floor.** A long rug breaks the straight sweep underfoot and gives the corridor a slower rhythm.
- **A picture, or several at intervals.** Art hung at eye level every couple of metres gives qi somewhere to settle as it travels. A turn in wall colour partway along does similar work.
- **A plant.** A healthy plant on a small console partway down the run softens the line and reads as living, settling growth in a space that is otherwise all transit.

One of these is usually enough for a moderate corridor. A very long one can take all three, spaced along its length, so the journey from one end to the other has two or three resting points rather than a single uninterrupted dash.

## Doors that open fully

The last thing to check in a hallway is the doors that open off it.

A door is meant to open all the way. The tradition uses a simple baseline: every door should swing fully to ninety degrees without catching on anything. A door blocked partway, whether by a coat hook loaded with coats behind it, a piece of furniture nudged too close, or a heap of shoes in the swing arc, is a door that only ever half-welcomes you into the room beyond. Clear whatever stops it and let it open clean.

Two further door notes belong to corridors specifically:

- **Doors facing each other across the hall.** Where two doors sit directly opposite on a narrow corridor, the tradition calls it the quarrelling-doors pattern and treats the two rooms as set against one another. You do not need to rebuild anything. Keep both closed when the rooms are not in use, or place a small visual break between them so the eye does not run straight from one into the other.
- **A door at the end of a long run.** If a single door closes off the far end of a long straight corridor, that door is taking the full push of the accelerated qi. The runner, picture, or plant that slows the run protects it; so does simply keeping that end door closed when the room behind it is resting.

## Where this leaves you

A hallway asks less of you than any room in the home, and the tradition is plain about that. You do not need it on a favourable direction. You only need it to do its one job well: carry movement from room to room without pinching it, without dimming it, and without letting it rush.

So walk your corridors once with these five questions. Is the floor clear. Is it well lit, every bulb warm and working. Does anything long and straight need a runner, a picture, or a plant to slow it. Do the doors open fully. And do any two doors face off across the hall. Most hallways need only one or two small corrections, and all of them are the kind you can finish in an afternoon.

## The staircase file

Stairs are the home's vertical artery, and the tradition reads them structurally. A stair facing the front door needs a break in the line of sight: a faceted crystal at the foot, art at eye level on the landing, or a runner that slows the eye, because a direct view up or down a flight reads as qi escaping before it settles. Two faults that placement cannot fully cure, only soften with planting at the base and consistent light: the spiral stair, which the tradition reads as a drill through the home's volume, and any stair at the exact centre of the floor plan. Along the corridor itself, doors should not face each other squarely; the tradition calls them quarrelling doors.

And one money reading with a refreshingly literal cure: gaps between treads, or loose ill-fitting steps, are read as value slipping through the openings, the staircase as a leaky wallet. The remedy is carpentry, not a charm. Fix the treads.
