# Your balcony, {{firstName}}

A balcony, a terrace, even a narrow ledge with room for one chair: the tradition treats this as the home's *breathing edge*. It is the seam where inside meets outside, where light and air arrive before anything else does, and where the home gets to exhale. Most readings spend their time on the rooms you live inside. This one steps to the threshold and looks out.

For you, {{firstName}}, with Kua number {{kuaNumber}} in the {{kuaGroup}} group, the balcony is worth reading the same way you read any other corner: by the directions it opens toward, and by what you put there.

## The breathing edge

The tradition reads a home as something that needs to draw breath. Air moves in, settles, circulates, and moves out again, and the balcony is one of the main places that exchange happens. A door or a window onto open air, used and kept clear, is treated as the lungs of the dwelling working as they should. A balcony sealed behind a wall of stored boxes is read the other way: the breath caught, the exchange stalled.

This is the first thing to notice about your outdoor edge. Before any question of direction, before plants or furniture, the tradition asks a plain question. *Does air actually move through here?* A balcony you step onto, where the door opens easily and the light comes in, reads as a living edge. A balcony you only see through glass, used as cold storage, reads as a held breath.

Three things the tradition associates with a healthy edge:

- **Light.** The balcony is often the brightest plane the home owns. Light is treated as the active, daytime quality, and an edge that lets it in freely is read as feeding the rooms behind it.
- **Air.** Open the door for a few minutes and the inside air pool refreshes faster through two openings than through one. The tradition and plain physics agree here.
- **A little green.** One or two living plants on the edge are read as the wood quality the tradition associates with growth and renewal, placed exactly where it belongs: at the meeting of house and sky.

## A calm corner, oriented to you

Most balconies have room for one chair and a small surface. That single sitting corner is where your own directions come in.

The tradition gives different directions different jobs. Your anchor direction, **{{fuWeiDir}}**, is the one it associates with stillness and composure: the direction to face when you want the mind to settle rather than race. Your health direction, **{{tianYiDir}}**, is the one it associates with rest and recovery. For a balcony corner meant for quiet sitting, either is a natural orientation.

The move is simple, and it is the chair you turn, not the view. Place the seat so that *your body faces* {{fuWeiDir}} when you want a few minutes of quiet, or {{tianYiDir}} when the corner is mostly for resting and unwinding. The surface, the railing, the plant pot can sit wherever the space allows. What the system reads is where you, sitting, are looking.

A short way to test it:

- Sit in the corner as it is now and note which way your body faces.
- Check that against your own directions. If you are already opening toward {{fuWeiDir}}, {{tianYiDir}}, your relationship direction {{yanNianDir}}, or your generating direction {{shengQiDir}}, the corner is well placed and you change nothing.
- If you are facing one of your four cautious directions ({{huoHaiDir}}, {{wuGuiDir}}, {{liuShaDir}}, or {{jueMingDir}}), try rotating the chair a quarter-turn so your body opens toward a supportive one instead.

## The fixed sector underneath

Separate from your personal directions, every balcony also sits in a fixed compass sector of the home, and the tradition hangs a life-area on each sector that is the same for every household. That mapping is fixed by the compass, not by you, so the sensible move is to look it up for your own floor plan rather than assume which sector your balcony falls in or what the tradition pins to it.

Because the sector is fixed but your directions are personal, the work is to *check one against the other*. Find which compass sector your balcony falls in. Then look at whether that same direction is one of your four supportive directions or one of your four to handle with care.

- If your balcony's sector is also one of your supportive directions, the tradition reads the corner as doubly aligned for you, and a calm, well-tended edge there is worth the attention.
- If your balcony's sector is one of your cautious directions, the reading is not a warning to abandon it. It points you to treat the space gently: keep it especially clear and cared for, let the light and air do their work, and lean on the plants and the calm rather than crowding it.

Either way the underlying instruction is the same. The sector tells you which life-area the tradition hangs on that corner; your own directions tell you how the corner sits with you personally. Neither one decides for the other.

## Cared for, not a dumping ground

The single most common thing that happens to a balcony is that it quietly becomes the place everything goes to be forgotten. The drying rack, the bag of soil, the chair with the broken leg, the box that never made it to the basement. The tradition reads this plainly: an edge meant to breathe, packed with stalled objects, stops being an edge and becomes a blockage at the very mouth where the home meets the outside.

You do not need a renovation to correct this. You need the edge cleared and kept clear.

- **Clear it once, properly.** Anything that does not belong on a balcony, move off it. What earns a place: somewhere to sit, somewhere to set a cup, one or two living plants, and clear floor to stand on.
- **Keep the view out open.** A clear line from the chair to the open air or the sky is part of what the tradition reads as the edge doing its job. Plants frame the view; they should not wall it off. If your outlook is onto a blank facing wall or a hard corner pointed at you, a trailing plant or a light screen softens it without sealing it.
- **Tend what lives there.** A thriving plant is read as the wood quality alive; a dead or struggling one is read as the opposite, and is the first thing to replace or remove. Sweep the floor, wipe the rail, let the daylight in. A cared-for edge is a different object from a neglected one, and the tradition weights the *cared-for* far more heavily than the expensive.

## A move to try

The easiest first move is the chair, because it turns. Step onto your balcony, sit in the corner, and notice which way you face. If it is one of your supportive directions, leave it. If not, rotate the seat so your body opens toward {{fuWeiDir}} for quiet or {{tianYiDir}} for rest. Then clear one thing off the edge that does not belong there, and put one living plant where it can catch the light.

That is the whole practice: an edge that breathes, a corner that faces a direction that supports you, and a space kept cared for rather than crowded. The balcony asks very little. Give it light, air, a little green, and a clear view out, and the tradition reads it as the home breathing the way it is meant to.

## The garden, miniaturised

The classical garden composes four things in balance: water, stone, living plants, and a place for people. A garden of plants alone reads as incomplete; the stone supplies the earth that balances the wood. For a balcony, the recipe miniaturises without losing a single part: **one water element** (a bowl, a small self-contained fountain), **one stone** (a single feature pebble or small statue), **living plants**, and **a seat you actually use**, because a garden lived in circulates qi while one only looked at stagnates. If the balcony opens to a wide view or open water, a rail planter or a framed sightline gives the eye a near anchor against all that distance. Four objects, one chair, and the smallest garden in the tradition is complete.
