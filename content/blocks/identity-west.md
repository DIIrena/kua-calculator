# Who you are, {{firstName}}

Pick an evening. Any evening. You walk into the room you walk into at the end of the day, and the warm lamp is doing the thing the overhead light cannot do, and a chair or a corner is doing what chairs and corners do for you, and you settle in without thinking about it, and you notice you feel *right*.

Then pick another evening. Same room, same lamp, same intention. You cannot settle. You shift. The cushion is wrong. The angle is wrong. You stand up after eight minutes and move to a different room because the room you were in is not letting you in. The night proceeds anyway.

Feng shui has a name for the gap between those two evenings. Or rather, it has an entire system that tries to explain why one set of conditions lets you settle, and another set of conditions keeps you on your feet, and why the same chair in the same lamp can do both depending on a handful of small things you might never have thought to notice.

The name of the system is **the Eight Mansions**. The single most useful number it gives you is your **Kua**.

## What the Kua actually is

Your Kua is a single digit between one and nine, with no five, calculated from the year you were born and your gender on the day of your birth. It is not your astrology. It is not a fortune. It is a *coordinator*.

Think of it this way. There are eight compass directions a person can face. Most of the time you do not notice which one you are facing, because most of the time it does not seem to matter. The chair faces the lamp. The desk faces the wall.

Feng shui's claim, said simply, is that for any given person, four of those eight directions are doing one kind of work, and four are doing another. The Kua tells you which four are which.

If that sounds like a tarot reading translated into compass language, it is worth knowing that the Eight Mansions has been the standard structured framework in Classical Chinese practice for around four hundred years, and the underlying yin-yang and trigram cosmology it rests on is older than that by another fifteen centuries. The system did not survive by accident. It survived because households that used it found it gave them something useful to *decide* with: where to put a bed, which chair the head of the family should take at dinner, where to put the safe.

It is, in short, a decision tool. It does not predict your future. It gives you a non-arbitrary way to choose between four otherwise equivalent options. That is more useful than people give it credit for.

You are Kua {{kuaNumber}}. You are West group. That is the door into the rest.

## What West group means

The eight Kua numbers split into two groups. West group contains 2, 6, 7, and 8. East group contains 1, 3, 4, and 9. Yours is West. The split is the single most consequential division in the system: a direction that is the best in the world for a West group person can be the worst in the world for an East group person sleeping in the same bed.

The four favourable directions for West group are:

- **Southwest**, the direction of earth and relationships.
- **West**, the direction of metal and harvest.
- **Northwest**, the direction of metal and helpful people.
- **Northeast**, the direction of earth and knowledge.

The four to be cautious of, by elimination, are north, east, southeast, and south. The system pairs them with specific qualities and we will name each in turn. What matters on this page is that you sit on the *gathering* side of the compass.

Look at the four good ones again. Two of them are earth, two of them are metal. Earth holds. Earth ripens. Earth is the unhurried direction that lets a thing become finished. Metal refines. Metal cuts away the rough. Metal carries water but is itself the harvest after the growing is done. None of your four favourable directions is the direction of new shoots. None of them is the direction of running brooks. You are someone the tradition reads as oriented towards *completion*.

That does not mean every West group person is slow. Plenty of West group people move quickly. What it means is that the qualities that feed you are qualities of *gathering*: collecting what you have already grown, refining what is already there, deepening relationships rather than starting new ones, finishing the work, holding the harvest.

<p class="pull-quote">You do your best work pointing into one of these four directions: <strong>Southwest, West, Northwest, Northeast</strong>.</p>

The opposite of gathering is not action. The opposite of gathering is *expansion*, which is what East group thrives on. East group people grow. West group people gather. Both are necessary. Both are right when they are right. The trouble is when a West group person tries to live as an East group person, or the reverse, and ends up exhausted, and cannot name why.

## What West group feels like, lived

You probably know this already without needing a system to tell you. The desks where you have done your best work, call up the memory of one of them, almost certainly faced west or northwest. The bed you slept best in, in the bedroom that felt most like rest rather than just absence-of-day, almost certainly had the headboard pointing west or northeast. The chair you read in last winter, the one you came back to night after night, was probably angled so your body opened towards one of these four directions.

It is not luck. The gathering directions are the directions your particular nervous system seems to settle into. The tradition has known this for two thousand years.

There is a felt-quality test you can try right now, and the test is older than the system that explains it. Stand up. Face whichever wall has the heaviest piece of furniture on it. Notice what your body is doing. Now turn ninety degrees. Notice again. The cheaper version of this test is the one most people accidentally run when they rearrange furniture, sit at the new desk, and within a week realise they cannot work there, and rearrange it back.

The grand version of the test is harder. It is asking yourself, honestly, whether the home you live in has the four West group sectors as its bright, used, alive sectors, or as its closets and laundry rooms. If your alive sectors are the four gathering ones, you are running with the wind on your back. If your alive sectors are the four growth ones, you are running with the wind in your face, and the wind has been there so long you have stopped noticing it.

This book cannot rearrange your home. It can hand you the compass.

## The Kua and the elements

A short detour, because it makes the rest of the book easier to read.

The five elements of Chinese metaphysics - wood, fire, earth, metal, water - each have a direction, a colour family, and a season. Two of them are associated with West group's favourable sectors. The other three are not.

- **Earth** is your southwest and your northeast. Beige, terracotta, cream, ochre, the colour of clay before it is fired. Late summer. Settled. Holding.
- **Metal** is your west and your northwest. White, grey, silver, gold, the colour of the harvest moon. Autumn. Refined. Cut and finished.

The three elements *not* on your good list are wood (the colour family of green, the season of spring), water (the colour of dark blue and black, the season of winter), and fire (red, summer). Each is real and necessary. Each is the right element for a particular task in a particular room. But each of them sits in your four cautious directions, and the tradition has a reason for that. Wood pushes. Water dissolves. Fire burns. Those qualities are not what your nervous system gathers around.

A house decorated wholly in spring greens and forest browns might look full of life in a magazine; for a West group person living in it day after day, it can feel like *trying to nap in a meadow*.

That does not mean strip the green out of your home. It means know what every colour and direction in your home is doing for you, and choose. The next ten pages give you the precise tools.

## Sharing a home with someone of a different group

This question comes up so often it deserves its own paragraph. About half of all couples are mixed: one West, one East. Your favourable directions are your partner's unfavourable directions, and the reverse, almost exactly.

The classical practitioner's answer is that you do not optimise the whole home for one of you. You optimise the *function* for the person whose use of it matters most. The shared bed orients to whichever direction works for the partner whose sleep is hardest to protect. The shared desk, if there is one, orients to whichever partner uses it for the more demanding work. The cook's stove orients to the cook. The dining table seats each of you in your own best direction.

In practice this is less of a compromise than it sounds. Most homes have more directions than you have people. Most rooms have more usable corners than you have couches. The work is *naming* what each room is for and then giving each room to one of you. The kitchen can be your partner's room. The reading chair can be yours. The book that takes a side and pretends the other partner does not exist is the book that ruins a marriage; the book that puts a name on every direction is the book that lets two people live together honestly.

*The Couple Compatibility Compass* takes the analysis further. It reads the two of you together. For now, it is enough to know that the half of the compass that is yours is not the half that is your partner's, and that the home is large enough for both of you.

## The four qualities, named

Each of your four favourable directions carries a traditional name and a quality. We will not lay them out as a table; you will read each one its own full chapter in the pages that follow. What matters here is that the names exist.

- **Sheng Qi** - your generating direction. The strongest of the four. Where you go when you want to push something forward.
- **Tian Yi** - your healing direction. The most common choice for the bedroom. Where you go when you need to rest, recover, or steady yourself.
- **Yan Nian** - your relationship direction. Where you sit with someone you love, or with someone you need to make peace with.
- **Fu Wei** - your anchor. The mildest of the four. Where you go to *be still and think*.

Your four cautious directions are Huo Hai, Wu Gui, Liu Sha, and Jue Ming. We will spend less time on them but we will not pretend they are not there. A book that ignored a person's harder directions would be a book that lied to its reader. You will read each of them in their own short page.
