# Before the compass: five checks

The classical teachers had a strict order of operations: **form first, compass second**. Before anyone asked which direction a bed should point, they checked what the room was doing to the body that slept in it. The reference books never hand out a direction without these checks around it, and neither should this one. Walk your home once with this list before you apply the chapters ahead; every direction you choose afterwards will sit on solid ground.

## 1. Take the command position

At the desk, never sit with your back to the door. Turn the chair so a wall backs you and the door falls somewhere in your view; if the only workable spot puts a window at your back, hang a good heavy curtain and keep it drawn while you work. This rule outranks direction: a favourable facing with the door behind you is a worse seat than a neutral facing in command of the room. When you can have both - wall at your back, favourable direction ahead - you have the best seat the tradition knows how to give.

## 2. Give the bed a solid back

The headboard wants a solid wall behind it: not a window, not a door, not a flimsy partition that carries every sound. Choose the quietest, most restful room you have for sleeping, and let the bed be the most important object in it. Every direction chapter that talks about the headboard assumes this check has already passed; a bed pointed perfectly at your best direction but backed by a rattling window has failed the earlier, more important test.

## 3. Keep both sides of a shared bed open

A couple's bed should be approachable from both sides, one side per partner, each with its own small surface for the lamp and the book. A double bed pushed lengthwise against a wall gives one partner a wall where an approach should be, and the tradition reads that as one person boxed in - which is also, unmystically, just true. Solo readers keeping the second side open will recognise this rule from the Yan Nian chapter: a room set up for the relationship you want is a room that is ready.

## 4. Point mirrors away from the bed

Keep mirrors out of the line of the bed, especially facing it. The tradition links a bed-facing mirror to friction and restlessness between partners; the plain reading is that a mirror doubles every movement and every gleam of light in the one room whose whole job is stillness. A mirror inside a wardrobe door, or one that simply faces another way, satisfies both readings. If it cannot move, cover it at night; a cloth over a mirror is the oldest bedroom cure there is.

## 5. Clear the arrows and the beams

Look at where you sleep and where you sit longest, and check what points at you. A sharp corner of a wardrobe aimed at the pillow, a shelf edge cutting toward the desk chair: the tradition calls these poison arrows, and the remedy is to angle the furniture, soften the edge, or move the seat out of the line. Then look up. Working, cooking, or sleeping under an exposed beam is read as pressure on whatever happens beneath it; shift the furniture out from under it where you can, and where you cannot, a canopy or a visually solid ceiling treatment softens the line.

That is the whole checklist, and it costs nothing but one slow walk through your home. The compass work in the next eight chapters is the fine tuning. This page is the foundation it tunes.
