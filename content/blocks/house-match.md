# Does your home match you, {{firstName}}?

Here is a question almost nobody thinks to ask, and it changes how you read everything else in this book: **your house has a Kua too.** The same eight trigrams that sorted you into the {{kuaGroup}} group also sort every building into a group of its own. Which means there is a match to check: you and the home you live in are either playing for the same team or politely working across the line. This chapter shows you how to find out, with the compass skills you already have.

## First, find your home's facing

Every compass technique past the Kua number needs two bearings for the building itself: the **facing**, the direction it presents to the world, and the **sitting**, the direction where its back rests. The two are always exactly opposite, 180 degrees apart, so determine one and you have both.

Popular sources will tell you the facing is "the direction of the front door." That is right often enough to be dangerous. The field method practitioners actually use is a short ranked checklist:

1. **The main door decides in most cases.** In roughly nine houses out of ten, the main entrance sits on the facing side, and the door's outward bearing is the facing direction. Stand in the doorway looking out, take the bearing with the three-reading protocol from the measuring chapter, and you are usually done.
2. **When the door is ambiguous, follow the life.** The facing side is the building's most active face: the heaviest traffic, the largest openings, the widest views, the street. Living rooms cluster on the facing side; bedrooms and kitchens gather on the sitting side. A house faces where the life is, not where the letterbox is.
3. **Know the famous exception.** A home whose door faces the street but whose entire living orientation, the windows, the terrace, the main rooms, opens to a view on the opposite side (the ocean, the valley) is the classic **view house**, and lineages genuinely disagree about whether it faces the door or the view. If yours is one, pick a convention, note it on your Room Map, and stay consistent. This book will not pretend the disagreement away.
4. **The tie-break is the sector test.** If the door sits at an angle to the main wall, measure both bearings. Landing in the same narrow sub-sector, the door's bearing stands. Landing in different ones, the ambiguity is real; choose deliberately rather than silently.

## From sitting to House Trigram

Turn your facing bearing around, 180 degrees, and you have the sitting direction. The trigram of the **sitting** direction is the House Trigram, the building's own Kua. A house that sits north (facing south) is a Kan house. A house that sits northwest is a Qian house. The same eight trigrams as yours, doing the same work.

And the eight house types divide exactly the way people do:

- **East houses** sit north, south, east, or southeast.
- **West houses** sit northeast, southwest, west, or northwest.

## The match

Now the payoff. The tradition's baseline reads simply: **an East-group person in an East house, or a West-group person in a West house, is the auspicious arrangement.** You are a {{kuaGroup}}-group reader; the sitting directions above tell you in one compass reading whether the building is on your team.

There is a lovely detail hiding in the matched case. In a house that shares your group, your Fu Wei direction, your personal anchor, tends to fall in the house's own sitting quadrant, the quadrant its back rests in. Stability meeting stability: the tradition's way of saying that a matched house feels like home base because, structurally, it is.

## If it does not match

First, breathe: a cross-group pairing is **livable**, and the tradition has always said so. What it asks of you is precision rather than luck. In a matched house the building's own grain quietly supports you; in a mismatched one, your personal directions inside it matter *more*, not less. The bed's wall, the chair's facing, the desk's quadrant: the placements this book has already taught you become the main instruments, and you now know exactly how to play them.

Two working rules for shared and rented homes:

- **A household spanning both groups** weights the match toward the person the classical convention calls the head of household, in practice the main earner or the person whose work anchors the home, while every member keeps their own favourable orientations in the rooms they personally anchor.
- **Renters** should feel quietly smug here: you never chose the building, but the room-as-microcosm rule means your eight directions exist at full strength inside any room you control. A mismatched building with a well-played bedroom beats a matched one used carelessly.

## Practitioner tip

The most practical use of this chapter is the one you do from the pavement. House-hunting with this system is mostly about the sitting direction, and you can take that bearing from the street before you ever step inside. Two homes shortlisted, one sits southeast and one sits northwest: a {{kuaGroup}}-group buyer now knows which viewing to attend with higher hopes, and it cost one compass reading. The reading never overrules a survey, a school district, or a good price. It is one more instrument, and you carry it in your pocket.
