# Your garage, {{firstName}}

A garage is the plainest room you own. Nobody asks it to be pretty. It holds the car, the tools, the bags of things you have not decided about yet. The tradition does not treat it the way it treats a bedroom or a kitchen, and that is the point worth understanding first.

## A heavy, working space

In the language of the eight directions, a garage is *yang* in use and *yin* in mood. It is full of metal, weight, fuel, and stored stillness. The tradition reads it as a utility space, not a place where you live, sleep, or eat, so the rules that govern your favourable directions matter far less here than they do in the rooms you occupy.

That makes a garage a sensible home for a cautious sector. Your four directions to handle with care - {{huoHaiDir}}, {{wuGuiDir}}, {{liuShaDir}}, and {{jueMingDir}} - are the ones the tradition would rather you not point a bed or a desk toward. A garage asks nothing of you for hours at a time. If your garage or outbuilding happens to sit in one of those four sectors of your plot, the tradition reads that as a reasonable match: you have given a heavy, low-demand function to a direction you were told to treat gently anyway.

This is not a reward and not a fix. It is a placement logic. A utilitarian room in a cautious sector is simply a quieter problem than a bedroom in the same spot.

## Ordered, not a black hole

The real risk in a garage is not its compass position. It is what it becomes. Left alone, a garage turns into the place where everything goes to be forgotten: the broken thing you meant to repair, the boxes from the last move, the equipment for a hobby you stopped. The tradition reads dense, untouched storage as stagnant. Nothing moves through it, so the energy of the space sits still and heavy.

You do not need a philosophy to feel this. A garage you can walk into and find what you need reads as a working room. A garage you avoid because you cannot reach the far wall reads as a black hole, and the avoidance is the tell.

A few working habits keep it ordered:

- **Give every category a wall, a shelf, or a hook.** Tools together, sports gear together, seasonal storage together. Floor space is for the car and for walking, not for piles.
- **Keep the floor clear enough to cross.** A clear path from the door to the car to the far corner is the single most useful thing in the room.
- **Treat the unsorted box as a decision, not a resident.** One box of undecided things is normal. A wall of them, two years deep, is the black hole forming.
- **Drain the broken and the dead.** Empty paint tins, a bike with no wheels, the appliance that never got fixed. The tradition treats broken and discarded objects in long-term storage as the heaviest, stillest thing in a home.

None of this changes the direction the garage sits in. It changes whether the space works or sinks.

## The threshold into the home

The single sector of a garage that the tradition treats with real care is the door between the garage and the house. Many homes connect the two directly, so that the family's most-used everyday entrance is not the front door at all but the door from the garage into the kitchen, the hallway, or the mudroom.

That threshold matters because it is a mouth into the part of the home where you actually live. Whatever sits against it on the garage side is the first thing the home receives.

- **Keep the garage side of that door clean and clear.** No bin against it, no shoe heap, no leaning ladder. The door should open fully without striking anything.
- **Light the threshold on both sides.** A working bulb in the garage by that door, and a light just inside the home. The tradition reads a dark transition as a place where energy slows and stalls; the practical reading is that a dark step is where people trip.
- **Let the home side read as home, not as overflow garage.** When the garage is the daily entrance, the first three steps inside should land in a tidy space, not on a pile of returns, recycling, and gear waiting to go back out.

If the threshold falls in one of your four supportive directions - {{shengQiDir}}, {{tianYiDir}}, {{yanNianDir}}, or {{fuWeiDir}} - the tradition reads it as a reasonable everyday entrance, and a clean one is worth keeping clean. If it falls in one of your four cautious directions, that is not a crisis: it is the room's heaviest sector doing what it was always going to do. Keep it clear and well lit and you have handled it gently, which is all the tradition asks.

## Light and a clear path

Two things separate a working garage from a dead one, and neither depends on your Kua number.

The first is **light.** Most garages are under-lit by default: one weak bulb, often burnt out, in a room full of corners. The tradition reads chronic dimness as the condition under which a space goes stagnant. The plain version is that a dark room is a room you stop entering, and a room you stop entering is the room that fills with forgotten things. One bright, working light, and a second by the threshold door, changes the room more than any cure.

The second is a **clear path.** Qi, in this tradition, moves the way you move: it follows the open route through a space and stalls where the route is blocked. A garage where you can walk a clean line from the outer door to the inner door, around the car, is a garage the tradition reads as alive. A garage where you sidestep and climb is one it reads as choked. The path is also the safety check, the cleaning check, and the reality check, all at once.

## What to take away

- The garage is a heavy, utilitarian space, and the tradition is content to see it sit in one of your cautious directions - {{huoHaiDir}}, {{wuGuiDir}}, {{liuShaDir}}, {{jueMingDir}} - because a low-demand room asks little of a difficult sector.
- The genuine work is order. A sorted garage reads as working; a clutter black hole reads as stagnant. The unsorted box is a decision waiting, not a tenant.
- The threshold into the home is the sector that earns real care. Keep the garage side clean, light both sides, and let the home side read as home.
- Light and a clear path do more here than any placement. Check them against your own directions only at the threshold; everywhere else, the practical rule is the rule.

A garage will never be the room the tradition makes promises about. Kept ordered, lit, and crossable, it is simply a heavy job done well - and the door it shares with your home is the one square metre of it worth treating with the same care you give the rooms you live in.
