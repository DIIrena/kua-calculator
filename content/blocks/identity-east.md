# Who you are, {{firstName}}

Pick a morning. Any morning. You walk into the room you walk into first, and the light is hitting the surface just so, and something in the room is doing the small ordinary noise it makes (the kettle, the heater, the cat at the door), and you stand there for a second without doing anything in particular, and you notice you feel *right*.

Then pick another morning. Same room, same light, same routine. The air is heavier, or you are facing the other wall, or the same chair is at the wrong angle, and you cannot tell why, but the same room is doing something different to you. The day starts anyway. The day proceeds.

Feng shui has a name for the gap between those two mornings. Or rather, it has an entire system that tries to explain why one set of conditions makes you feel met, and a different set of conditions makes you feel *off*, and why the same room can do both depending on a handful of small things you might never have thought to notice.

The name of the system is **the Eight Mansions**. The single most useful number it gives you is your **Kua**.

Your Kua is {{kuaNumber}}. You are East group.

## What the Kua actually is

Your Kua is a single digit between one and nine, with no five, calculated from the year you were born and your gender on the day of your birth. It is not your astrology. It is not a fortune. It is a *coordinator*.

Think of it this way. There are eight compass directions a person can face. Most of the time you do not notice which one you are facing, because most of the time it does not seem to matter. The morning coffee is the same coffee. The keyboard is the same keyboard.

Feng shui's claim, said simply, is that for any given person, four of those eight directions are doing one kind of work, and four are doing another. The Kua tells you which four are which.

If that sounds like a tarot reading translated into compass language, it is worth knowing that the Eight Mansions has been the standard structured framework in Classical Chinese practice for around four hundred years, and the underlying yin-yang and trigram cosmology it rests on is older than that by another fifteen centuries. The system did not survive by accident. It survived because households that used it found it gave them something useful to *decide* with: where to put a bed, which chair the head of the family should take at dinner, which way the cook should face at the stove.

It is, in short, a decision tool. It does not predict your future. It gives you a non-arbitrary way to choose between four otherwise equivalent options. That is more useful than people give it credit for.

You are Kua {{kuaNumber}}. You are East group. That is the door into the rest.

## What East group means

The eight Kua numbers split into two groups. East group contains 1, 3, 4, and 9. West group contains 2, 6, 7, and 8. Yours is East. The split is the single most consequential division in the system: a direction that is the best in the world for an East group person can be the worst in the world for a West group person sleeping in the same bed.

The four favourable directions for East group are:

- **North**, the direction of water.
- **South**, the direction of fire.
- **East**, the direction of wood and morning sun.
- **Southeast**, the direction of wood and abundance, traditionally called the wealth direction.

The four to be cautious of, by elimination, are northeast, southwest, west, and northwest. The system pairs them with specific qualities and we will name each in turn. What matters on this page is that you sit on the *growth* side of the compass.

Look at the four good ones again. Three of them - water, wood, wood - are the elements the tradition associates with *motion*. Water flows. Wood grows. South is the recognition direction, where you are seen. None of them is a holding direction. None of them is about gathering things and keeping them still.

You are someone the tradition reads as oriented towards reaching. That does not mean every East group person is loud or extroverted. Plenty of East group people are quiet. What it means is that the qualities that feed you are qualities of *expansion*: learning new things, having long conversations, building one project on top of another, recognising and being recognised, generating rather than refining.

<p class="pull-quote">You do your best work pointing into one of these four directions: <strong>North, South, East, Southeast</strong>.</p>

The opposite of expansion is not stillness. The opposite of expansion is *containment*, which is what West group thrives on. West group people gather. East group people grow. Both are necessary. Both are right when they are right. The trouble is when an East group person tries to live as a West group person, or the reverse, and ends up exhausted, and cannot name why.

## What East group feels like, lived

You probably know this already without needing a system to tell you. The desks where you have done your best work, call up the memory of one of them, almost certainly faced east or southeast. The bed you slept best in, in the bedroom that felt most like rest rather than just absence-of-day, almost certainly had the headboard pointing north or east. The chair you read in last summer, where the afternoon flowed without effort, was probably angled so your body opened towards one of these four directions.

It is not luck. The growth directions are the directions your particular nervous system seems to settle into. The tradition has known this for two thousand years.

There is a felt-quality test you can try right now, and the test is older than the system that explains it. Pick any room you are in. Stand in the middle of it. Face whichever wall feels like the *front* of the room: usually the wall with the brightest window, but pick whatever wall feels primary. Notice what your body is doing. Now turn ninety degrees and face a different wall. Notice again. Most people are surprised the first time at how clearly the body picks one direction over another. The cheaper version of this test is the one most people accidentally run when they rearrange furniture, sit at the new desk, and within a week realise they cannot work there, and rearrange it back.

The grand version of the test is harder. It is asking yourself, honestly, whether the home you live in has the four East group sectors as its bright, used, alive sectors, or as its closets and laundry rooms. If your bright sectors are the four growth ones, you are running with the wind on your back. If your bright sectors are the four containment ones, you are running with the wind in your face, and the wind has been there so long you have stopped noticing it.

This book cannot rearrange your home. It can hand you the compass.

## The Kua and the elements

A short detour, because it makes the rest of the book easier to read.

The five elements of Chinese metaphysics - wood, fire, earth, metal, water - each have a direction, a colour family, and a season. Two of them are associated with East group's favourable sectors, and the third, fire, sits at the south.

- **Water** is your north. Cool, deep, downward-running, associated with career, learning, and intuition. Black, dark blue, the colour of the river at night. Winter.
- **Wood** is your east and your southeast. Green and brown, the colours of growing things. Spring. Vertical motion. The trees in the courtyard.
- **Fire** is your south. Bright red, the colour of recognition. Summer. The direction of being seen.

The two elements *not* on your good list are earth (the colour family of beige, terracotta, cream) and metal (white, grey, silver, gold). They sit in your four cautious directions, and the tradition has a reason for that. Earth holds. Metal refines. Both of those qualities are real and necessary, but they pull in the opposite direction from where you naturally generate. A house decorated wholly in beige and cream and silver might look serene in a magazine; for an East group person living in it day after day, it can feel like *running in sand*.

That does not mean strip the beige out of your home. It means know what every colour and direction in your home is doing for you, and choose. The next ten pages give you the precise tools.

## Sharing a home with someone of a different group

This question comes up so often it deserves its own paragraph. About half of all couples are mixed: one East, one West. Your favourable directions are your partner's unfavourable directions, and the reverse, almost exactly.

The classical practitioner's answer is that you do not optimise the whole home for one of you. You optimise the *function* for the person whose use of it matters most. The shared bed orients to whichever direction works for the partner whose sleep is hardest to protect. The shared desk, if there is one, orients to whichever partner uses it for the more demanding work. The cook's stove orients to the cook. The dining table seats each of you in your own best direction.

In practice this is less of a compromise than it sounds. Most homes have more directions than you have people. Most rooms have more usable corners than you have couches. The work is *naming* what each room is for and then giving each room to one of you. The kitchen can be your partner's room. The reading chair can be yours. The book that takes a side and pretends the other partner does not exist is the book that ruins a marriage; the book that puts a name on every direction is the book that lets two people live together honestly.

*The Couple Compatibility Compass* takes the analysis further. It reads the two of you together. For now, it is enough to know that the half of the compass that is yours is not the half that is your partner's, and that the home is large enough for both of you.

## The four qualities, named

Each of your four favourable directions carries a traditional name and a quality. We will not lay them out as a table; you will read each one its own full chapter in the pages that follow. What matters here is that the names exist.

- **Sheng Qi** - your generating direction. The strongest of the four. Where you go when you want to push something forward.
- **Tian Yi** - your healing direction. The most common choice for the bedroom. Where you go when you need to rest, recover, or steady yourself.
- **Yan Nian** - your relationship direction. Where you sit with someone you love, or with someone you need to make peace with.
- **Fu Wei** - your anchor. The mildest of the four. Where you go to *be still and think*.

Your four cautious directions are Huo Hai, Wu Gui, Liu Sha, and Jue Ming. We will spend less time on them but we will not pretend they are not there. A book that ignored a person's harder directions would be a book that lied to its reader. You will read each of them in their own short page.
