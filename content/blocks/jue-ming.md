# Your Jue Ming direction is {{direction}}

Jue Ming is the last of your four cautious directions and the one the tradition treats with the most care. The name translates as **"Total Loss"**, strong language inherited from the older texts. It is a traditional caution, not a verdict on your life or your home. For you, {{firstName}}, it points **{{direction}}**.

Say the practical truth early: a bedroom whose only workable layout points the bed at Jue Ming is *common*, and it is fine. Homes are built around plumbing, windows, and front doors, not around your Kua number. This chapter treats the direction with respect and then hands it back to you as a small, manageable thing.

## What this means

The full name is *Jue Ming* (<span lang="zh-Hant">絕命</span>). What it describes is a traditional association, not a forecast: the felt-quality of being slowly tired rather than rested, over **long exposures**, in the places where the body holds still for hours. That is why the caution attaches to the bed and the main work seat, and not to short visits. A bed is not made harmful by a wall. A desk is not made harder by a direction. The tradition is offering a *priority*, not a diagnosis: of the eight directions, this is the one it would site last.

Two things are true at once, and the chapter asks you to hold both. Of all the directions in your chart, this is the one most worth treating with care. And of all the directions in your chart, this is *still a direction*, the same kind of thing as the other seven.

## Give it this

- **The bathroom.** The canonical tenant: it contains and disposes, and asks no one to linger.
- **The storage room** or the deep cupboard that sits quiet.
- **The laundry corner, the coat hooks,** the shelf of things that wait until the weekend.

Most homes solve Jue Ming without anyone deciding to. Look at where {{direction}} falls on your Room Map; there is a fair chance the right thing is already sitting there.

## Keep off it

- **No bed**, while any other wall in the room can take the headboard.
- **No main desk facing it** for your daily working hours.
- **No evening chair.** The reading corner where you settle for hours belongs to your supportive half.

## When you cannot avoid it

Some readers do not have a choice, and this section is written for them. Work down the order. First, prefer any favourable wall for the headboard. If none works, prefer any of your *other three* cautious walls; the order is the whole point. If the bed truly must point {{direction}}, use the traditional softeners: a **solid headboard**, no slats and no gap above the pillow, which the tradition reads as a wall behind the head; and a screen or a piece of furniture that breaks the open sight-line across that wall if the room has space. Neither is a fix. Both are the next-best thing, and the next-best thing is what the tradition has always asked of itself. Then win the desk: give your working chair a favourable facing, because for a working adult the alert hours are the ones most worth aligning.

## Practitioner tip

When someone arrives worried because "the bed points at the worst one," I do two things in order. First the ordinary checks, because they outrank the compass: is the room dark, is it quiet, is the phone out of it, does the mattress deserve the blame. Then the priority work above, calmly, as a checklist rather than a rescue. What I never do is let the name do the talking. A reader who pays a few dollars for this book deserves to hear it plainly: Jue Ming is a corner, not a verdict, and the tradition's own remedy list is an afternoon of carpentry, not an exorcism.

## A real home

Sofia rents, which means the big levers are off the table. She is Kua 1, so her Jue Ming points southwest, and her rented bedroom offers exactly one bed position: headboard southwest. Buying was out; drilling was out. So she worked the renter's version of the protocol. A freestanding solid headboard leaned and secured against that wall, bought second-hand. A tall bookcase angled to break the long sight-line across the room. And the real win: her small desk moved under the window so her chair faces east, her Tian Yi, for the hours she works from home. When her lease ends, all three moves leave with her. The room the flat forced on her and the room she actually lives in are no longer the same room.

That is the last of your four cautious directions, and your full personal compass is now named: four the tradition reads as supportive, four it asks you to handle with care. The compass is yours, and the home is quieter than the names suggest.
