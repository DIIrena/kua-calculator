# Your Wu Gui direction is {{direction}}

You are at dinner. The food is good. The wine is fine. There is a question someone asks halfway through the main course, a small ordinary question, and the answer lands at a slightly wrong angle, and the next answer lands at a slightly wronger angle, and within four minutes the table is having a different conversation than the one it was having, and nobody knows quite when the turn happened. The chairs have not moved. The lighting has not changed. The same six people are at the same six seats. But the evening has tilted, and the rest of it will be spent gently righting itself, and on the way home, in the car, you and the person you came with will say to each other *that got weird, didn't it*, and neither of you will be able to say *why*.

That is Wu Gui weather. Not catastrophe. Not doom. Just the small reliable tilt that some rooms seem to have built into one of their corners.

## What Wu Gui means

The full name is *Wu Gui* (五鬼), which translates literally as "Five Ghosts." It is the second of your four cautious directions, and for you, {{firstName}}, it points {{direction}}.

The name is more theatrical than the phenomenon. Classical practitioners associate Wu Gui with arguments, miscommunication, lost items, and hidden friction; the direction the tradition reads as breeding *confusion and dispute*. The honest framing is gentler than the name suggests. A wall where conversations seem to consistently go sideways may have other reasons (acoustics, the angle of the light, the chair that puts one person's back to the door), but the tradition has been naming this pattern for centuries and it is worth paying attention to.

Wu Gui's failure mode is *friction*, not collapse. Things do not break in this corner. They just keep almost-breaking, in small repeated ways, in patterns nobody quite catalogues until somebody points it out.

## The lived test

You probably know this already. You have just been calling it something else.

There is a chair at the conference table at work that nobody volunteers for. It is not the worst chair. The worst chair has its back to the door and people avoid it openly. This chair is something else. It is the chair the room's energy gathers *against*. The person who ends up in it is the one whose point gets misheard, whose suggestion gets credited to someone else two minutes later, who is asked to repeat themselves twice in the same meeting. Everyone has been in that chair on a Tuesday and gone back to their desk vaguely annoyed without knowing what at.

There is a dining seat at your parents' house, or your sister's, where the difficult conversation seems to always happen. Not because the seat causes the conversation. Because the seat is *available* in the way the others, somehow, are not. The person who takes it gets the version of the evening where their job comes up.

There is a corner of the office where projects go to be slowly, quietly forgotten. The file lives there. The folder lives there. The plant lives there. Nobody decides to ignore the corner; the corner just *receives* the kind of attention things receive when they are filed and never reopened. Three months later somebody asks about the project and there is a small awkward pause.

The tradition has a name for the corner. The corner is Wu Gui.

## The hard thing

The way Wu Gui actually causes trouble in a home is specific, and worth naming.

People put their primary couch on this wall. The couch where they talk to their partner about money. The couch where the difficult phone calls happen. The couch where the disagreement that has been brewing for a fortnight finally lands. And then they wonder why, in *that* couch, the same conversation goes sideways every time, when in the kitchen the same conversation goes fine. They think it is the couch. It might be the couch. The tradition would say it is also the wall the couch is on.

The other failure mode is workplace. People who keep cycling through assistants, or contractors, or freelancers, and cannot name what keeps going wrong, sometimes have the workspace built on Wu Gui. The hire shows up. The hire seems good. Three months later the hire is gone for reasons that, in retrospect, are hard to summarise. Repeat. The system reads this as a tendency the room is supporting. It does not mean the people were bad. It means the *seat* was rough.

The third failure mode is the bed. A bed pointed at Wu Gui is a bed where the small irritations between two people in it tend not to resolve overnight. They linger. The morning arrives with the previous evening still in the air. It is, again, not catastrophe. It is *friction that does not metabolise the way it should*. Like a houseplant that does not quite die but does not quite thrive, for years, on a shelf that gets just-not-enough light.

## The fork

You have a few choices about this wall, and the tradition is unusually direct about them.

What to put on Wu Gui: laundry. Cleaning supplies. The utility cupboard. The hallway storage closet. The bookshelf for reference books you rarely open. The seasonal-decoration boxes. The closet you open twice a year to retrieve a winter coat. The corner where the vacuum lives. The shelf with the spare lightbulbs and the box of mismatched batteries and the small brass elephant your aunt sent you in 2008.

What *not* to put on Wu Gui: the bed, ever. The conference chair, if you have one. The dining seat for the head of household. The chair where you take difficult phone calls. The couch where the relationship conversations happen. The desk of any employee whose retention you would like to preserve. The reading chair, if the reading chair is also the chair where you process the day with someone you live with.

The pattern is consistent. Wu Gui is the wall for *low-stakes containment*. Things that do not need attention live here, and they live here gladly, because the tradition reads this corner as one that *absorbs* things rather than radiating them outward. Storage is its native function. Conversation is not.

If you cannot avoid the wall for an important function (the apartment is small, the conference room is the conference room, the bedroom has one bed-shaped slot and it is on Wu Gui), the tradition's standard moves are mild: a heavy piece of furniture between you and the wall, a closed cupboard rather than open shelves, calm earth-toned colour on that side of the room, nothing reflective. None of this is a fix. It is a *softening*. The full fix is to move the function.

## What's next

That is Wu Gui. The five ghosts that, on inspection, are mostly small reliable arguments and lost car keys and the meeting that did not quite resolve. Treat the wall as storage and most of what the tradition warns you about handles itself. Treat it as a couch and you may, six months in, find yourself wondering why the conversations on it never quite land right.
