# Your bathroom, {{firstName}}

Every home loses water somewhere, and the bathroom is where it leaves fastest. The tradition reads this room as a *draining* room: the place with the most active plumbing, the most moisture, and the most water moving out of the house. That single fact decides almost everything the tradition has to say about it. You do not activate a bathroom. You contain it.

Because the room drains, the tradition treats it differently from a bedroom or a desk. A bedroom or a working seat is somewhere you want to sit *in* one of your supportive directions. A bathroom is the rare case where the tradition is content to see it sit in one of your *cautious* directions instead. There is a logic to that, and it is worth understanding before you go looking at your own floor plan.

## Why a draining room suits a cautious direction

You carry four supportive directions and four to handle with care. The named cautious pair this reading leans on are **{{jueMingDir}}** and **{{wuGuiDir}}**, the two the tradition treats as the most demanding of your eight. In a room you sleep or work in, you would rather not face those. But a bathroom is not a room you orient your body in for hours. It is a room whose main job is to take things away.

So the tradition makes a quiet trade. A sector you would not want to spend the day facing is a sector you are content to *spend* on plumbing. If your bathroom happens to fall in **{{jueMingDir}}** or **{{wuGuiDir}}**, the tradition reads that as a reasonable, even tidy, arrangement: a draining function placed over a direction you were going to handle carefully anyway. The room is doing something useful with a square of the home you were not going to build a bed or a desk into.

The reverse is the case the tradition watches. A bathroom sitting squarely over one of your four supportive directions, your generating **{{shengQiDir}}**, your health **{{tianYiDir}}**, your relationship **{{yanNianDir}}**, or your anchor **{{fuWeiDir}}**, is read as a draining function placed on a direction you would rather keep strong. The tradition does not say this ruins anything. Most people cannot move a bathroom, and the system knows it. It says: where you had a choice, you would not have put the drain there, so be a little more deliberate about containing it.

## How to read your own bathroom

Walk to the room and work out which compass sector it sits in, relative to the centre of your home. Then check that sector against your own eight directions.

- If it lands in **{{jueMingDir}}** or **{{wuGuiDir}}**, or in your other two cautious directions, **{{huoHaiDir}}** or **{{liuShaDir}}**, the tradition reads the placement as well suited. The draining function and the careful direction agree. Keep the room contained and you have a configuration the system is happy with.
- If it lands in one of your four supportive directions, treat the room a little more attentively. Nothing here is a verdict, and nothing needs rebuilding. You simply lean harder on the containment habits below, because the tradition would rather not see a supportive direction drained casually.

This is the same move you make anywhere a fixed feature meets your personal map. You do not assume what a sector means in the abstract. You check the sector against *your* directions and let that decide how lightly or firmly to handle the room.

## The containment habits

The tradition's bathroom rules are short, old, and almost entirely practical. None of them is mysterious. Each is something you can do tonight.

**Keep the door closed.** A closed door is the simplest and most often repeated bathroom move there is. It isolates the draining room from the rooms around it and stops a bathroom from sitting in anyone's line of sight. A bathroom you can see straight down a hallway, from the front door, from the kitchen, or from a bed is read across the tradition as a small constant leak in the line of view. The cure is almost always the door itself: keep it shut. Where the door cannot stay shut, a curtain or a low screen breaks the sightline.

**Keep the lid down.** Closing the lid is the small daily version of closing the door. The tradition reads it as keeping the draining point covered when it is not in use. It costs nothing and it is the one habit worth making automatic.

**Keep the drains in good order.** A clean, free-running drain is part of the reading, not just the plumbing. Water that backs up, a tap that drips, a slow gurgle in the floor drain, the tradition treats these as a draining room that has stopped draining cleanly, which is exactly the state you do not want a draining room to be in. Clear the drains, fix the drip, keep the water leaving the way it should.

## Light, air, and dryness

A draining room is also a damp room, and the tradition wants it bright, aired, and dry rather than dim and shut. The three pull in the same direction.

- **Light.** Working light, not a single tired bulb. A bright bathroom reads as a cared-for room; a dim one reads as a neglected corner of the house. Warm-temperature bulbs suit an evening bathroom.
- **Air.** A run of the extractor fan, or an open window for a few minutes, moves the moisture out instead of letting it sit. A bathroom with no air movement holds its damp, and the tradition reads held damp as the room's least settled state.
- **Dryness.** Folded towels rather than damp ones on the floor, no standing water, a wiped surface. A humidity-tolerant plant, a pothos, a peace lily, a small fern, is a long-standing suggestion here: a touch of living wood in a water-heavy room, with the practical bonus that such plants tolerate the moisture well.

Where you keep all three, light on, air moving, surfaces dry, the tradition reads the room as a contained, well-kept drain rather than a stagnant one. That is the whole goal.

## What to avoid placing above a bathroom

Two siting patterns are worth knowing, for the rare times you actually have a choice, a renovation, a new layout, a decision about which room goes where upstairs.

A bathroom sitting directly **over a bed** is read as a draining room positioned above the place you spend a third of your life at rest. Where the layout can avoid it, the tradition prefers the bed not share that vertical line. Where it cannot, you do the ordinary thing: you keep the bathroom above contained, door closed, lid down, drains sound, and you do not treat the overlap as a sentence. Most people inherit their plumbing. The tradition asks for deliberate containment, not demolition.

A bathroom sitting directly **over a stove**, or over the kitchen, draws the same caution. The stove is read across the tradition as one of the most important fixtures in the home, tied to the household's nourishment, and a draining room placed directly above it is the kind of overlap the tradition would rather not see. Again: avoid it where you are choosing the layout; contain it well where you have inherited it.

Neither pattern is a thing you must fix at any cost. They are siting preferences for the moments you get to decide, and containment habits for every other moment.

## A move to try

Start with the door and the lid, because they cost nothing and you control them entirely. Close the bathroom door tonight and make a habit of closing the lid. Then take one slow look at the room: is the light working, is the air moving, are the surfaces dry? Fix whichever of those is weakest first.

Then, when you have a quiet minute, work out which sector your bathroom sits in and check it against your own directions. If it falls in **{{jueMingDir}}** or **{{wuGuiDir}}**, the tradition reads the placement as well chosen and asks only that you keep it contained. If it falls in one of your four supportive directions, lean a little harder on the same habits. Either way the work is the same small set of moves. This reading is a decision tool, not a discipline, and the bathroom is the room where the easiest moves carry most of the weight.
