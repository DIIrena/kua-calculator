# Your family corner, {{firstName}}

There is one corner of every home the tradition hands to family. It does not move from person to person. It does not shift with your Kua number. It sits, fixed, in the **East**, the same East for you, {{firstName}}, as for everyone else who has ever laid this map over a floor plan. What changes is what you put there, how you tend it, and whether your own directions happen to fall in agreement with it. That last part is where you come in, and this reading will get you there.

## What the East holds

In the eight-direction map, the East is the sector the tradition reads as the seat of *family, roots, and new growth*. It is tied to the household as a living thing: the people you share the home with, the line you come from, the wellbeing of the group as a whole, and the quiet, ongoing project of a family that keeps growing rather than going still.

The tradition gives the East three threads that braid together:

- **Family and ancestors.** The people present in the home now, and the line behind them. This is the corner the tradition associates with the bond between generations.
- **Roots and continuity.** The sense that the household has a foundation under it, something older than this year's worries.
- **New growth.** Not growth as a promise of more, but growth as the *quality* of a thing that is still alive and still moving. The East is read as the direction of the beginning of things, the upward push of spring.

Hold these as associations the system makes, not as outcomes it hands you. The East does not *give* you a settled family. The tradition simply reads this sector as the one that speaks to family, and treats a cared-for East as a corner kept in good order.

## Wood is its element

Every sector carries an element, and the East carries **wood**. This matters because wood is not a material here so much as a quality of motion: the energy of new growth pushing upward, of life and expansion, of a thing in the act of beginning. Spring is its season. Green is its colour. Tall, upright, columnar shapes express it.

That gives you a vocabulary for the corner. To speak to the East in the language the tradition uses, you reach for wood:

- **Living plants.** A healthy plant is the most direct expression of wood there is. The tradition reads a thriving green plant in the East as wood doing exactly what wood does, which is growing.
- **Upright, natural materials.** Untreated timber, bamboo, paper, cotton. Anything tall and natural leans the corner toward wood.
- **Greens and soft browns.** Colour carries an element as surely as material does.

A caution the tradition is careful about: the plant has to be *alive*. A wilting or dying plant is not a small version of a healthy one; it reads as the opposite of what wood is for. If a plant in this corner is struggling, the tradition's instinct is to tend it or replace it, not to leave it sitting there as decoration. The same logic asks you to keep dried flowers, bare twigs, and artificial greenery out of the role of the main wood expression. They can sit elsewhere; the family corner asks for something living.

## Family photos, living plants, and a corner that is cared for

Beyond the element itself, the East is the natural home for the objects that carry family meaning. **Family photographs** belong here in the tradition's reading: images of the people in the household, of the generations behind them, of the group as it actually is. The corner is treated as a place to gather what represents the family rather than scatter it across the home.

The practical instruction is plain, and it is the same instruction the tradition gives for every sector that matters: keep it cared for.

- **Keep it clear.** A family corner buried under storage, clutter, or things-with-nowhere-else-to-go is read as a corner gone stagnant. The East occupied by a heap of boxes is one of the more commonly flagged arrangements, precisely because family is a thread the tradition would rather see active than buried.
- **Keep it alive.** The living plant, tended. The photographs, dusted and current rather than frozen a decade back.
- **Keep it intact.** Cracked frames, broken objects, and dead growth all pull against what the corner is for. Mending or removing them is the small, ongoing work the tradition associates with a cared-for East.

None of this is dramatic. The tradition treats the family corner the way you would treat a garden bed: not a one-time fix, but a thing you return to. The reading is a decision tool, not a discipline, and the East asks for attention rather than perfection.

## The Kua overlay: check your own directions

Here is where your personal map meets the fixed one, {{firstName}}.

The East is the same sector for everyone, but your eight personal directions are yours alone, set by your Kua number ({{kuaNumber}}) and your membership in the {{kuaGroup}} group. The two maps sit on top of each other, and the interesting question is whether they agree in the East.

You cannot assume they do. The fixed family sector and your personal directions are calculated differently, so the only way to know is to check. Take your four supportive directions, which are your generating direction {{shengQiDir}}, your health direction {{tianYiDir}}, your relationship direction {{yanNianDir}}, and your anchor direction {{fuWeiDir}}, and look at where East falls among them.

- **If East is one of your four supportive directions**, the fixed family corner and your personal map are pointing the same way. The tradition reads that overlap as a corner that is doubly in agreement with you, and it is worth leaning into: this is a natural place to keep the plant, the photographs, the wood.
- **If East is one of your four cautious directions**, meaning your {{huoHaiDir}}, {{wuGuiDir}}, {{liuShaDir}}, or {{jueMingDir}}, the maps disagree here. That does not cancel the family corner; the fixed sector is still the fixed sector. The tradition's instinct is simply to treat it *gently*: keep it clean and cared for, keep the wood healthy, and avoid making this corner the spot where you sit for hours or sleep. Tend it, but do not over-activate it.

There is a particular thread worth tracing: your **health direction, {{tianYiDir}}**. The East carries the household's wellbeing as one of its associations, and your Kua map carries a personal health direction as one of yours. They are two different things, one a fixed sector and one a personal bearing, and they only reinforce each other if {{tianYiDir}} happens to be East. If it is, the tradition reads the alignment as a strong one for the family corner, and you can let the East stand for both threads. If your {{tianYiDir}} points elsewhere, that is the bearing the tradition would turn the body toward when resting; the East still holds the household's family thread, but the personal health bearing lives somewhere else in the home. Knowing which case you are in is the whole point of checking.

## A move to try

The easy first move for the East is the plant, because it asks almost nothing of you. Find the eastern side of the home, clear a small space, and put one healthy living plant there, something green, upright, and genuinely thriving. Set a family photograph beside it if you have one to hand. Then check East against your four supportive directions and your {{tianYiDir}}, and let the answer tell you whether to lean in or tend gently.

The tradition holds the family corner in the East, {{firstName}}, and it has held it there the whole time. What it asks of you is small and ongoing: keep it living, keep it clear, and check whether your own map agrees with it.
