# Your kitchen, {{firstName}}

The kitchen is where the home is fed. Of all the rooms a feng shui reading looks at, this one tends to come first or close to it, because the central object, the stove, is treated as the place the household is sustained. The tradition reads a kitchen the way it reads few other rooms: not by mood or palette, but by the working condition of the thing that cooks.

So this reading starts there, with the stove itself, and then works outward to where you stand, what faces what, and how the room flows.

## The stove comes first

Before any direction, before any clever placement, the tradition asks one plain thing of the stove: that it be clean and in working order. A burner that does not light, a surface crusted with months of cooked-on oil, a hood that no longer pulls, the tradition reads each of these as a kitchen working at half strength. The fixture itself is the thing being read, so the fixture itself is the first move.

This is the least mystical instruction in the whole reading and the one most worth doing tonight:

- **Clean the stove.** A polished cooktop is, to the tradition, a different object from a neglected one. No cure substitutes for this.
- **Keep it in repair.** Every burner lighting, the ignition steady, the extractor running when you cook. A stove that half-works reads as a home half-fed.
- **Use it.** A stove that never gets switched on reads as a cold hearth. The tradition treats regular, ordinary cooking as the room doing its job.

None of this promises you anything. It is simply the baseline the rest of the kitchen reading sits on. Get the stove right first, then arrange around it.

## Stand where you can see the door

The single oldest instruction for a kitchen is about where the cook stands. The tradition wants the person at the stove to be able to see the doorway without turning their body to do it. This is the command position, the same principle the reading applies to a bed or a desk, scaled down to a single working spot.

The logic is plain rather than magical. A cook with their back to the entrance is in a low, constant state of alert, since someone could enter unannounced, and the tradition reads that low background tension as the wrong note for the room that feeds the house. A cook who can see who comes in, on the other hand, works settled.

Many modern stoves are set against a wall, which puts the cook's back to the room. Where you cannot move the stove, the long-standing remedy is reflective:

- **Hang a small mirror or a polished metal panel** above or beside the cooktop so the doorway shows in it. Seeing the entrance by reflection satisfies the same principle the direct view would.
- **Reposition where you can.** If your kitchen has an island or a second working surface that does let you face the room, favour it for the focused, attentive parts of cooking.

The point is the line of sight, not the literal facing. Give yourself the door, directly or by reflection, and the command-position rule is met.

## Keep fire and water apart

The stove is fire. The sink, the refrigerator, and the dishwasher are water. The tradition does not want fire and water sitting directly against each other or staring each other down, and where the layout gives you any choice, it asks you to keep a little separation between the two.

In practice this means two things:

- **Side by side, touching.** A stove immediately beside the sink on the same run is the most common modern version of the conflict, especially in galley and single-wall kitchens. The tradition associates an unbroken fire-and-water adjacency with a friction in the room, and it asks for a buffer between them.
- **Directly facing.** A stove that looks straight across a narrow kitchen at the sink or the fridge sets fire and water against each other across the gap. Where you can break that direct facing, the reading prefers it.

The mediator the tradition reaches for is wood, because in the five-element cycle wood sits between water and fire and passes one into the other. You do not need to rebuild the kitchen to apply this:

- A **wooden chopping board** left standing between the stove and the sink.
- A small **potted herb** such as basil, mint, or rosemary on the counter in the gap.
- A length of clear **working surface** between the two, kept as a buffer rather than crowded with appliances.

If your layout simply does not allow the separation, and many do not, place one of these wood elements in the line between fire and water and treat the conflict as softened rather than solved. The reading is a decision tool, not a demand for a renovation.

## Where your own directions come in

Everything above is the same for every household. Your personal directions add one optional layer on top, for the parts of kitchen work that are focused and deliberate rather than quick.

You belong to the {{kuaGroup}} group, with Kua number {{kuaNumber}}. Two of your supportive directions are worth knowing here: your generating direction, **{{shengQiDir}}**, and your health direction, **{{tianYiDir}}**. Where you have a genuine choice about which way your body faces during attentive kitchen work, such as prepping at an island, standing at a board for a long task, or working at a counter you could orient either way, the tradition suggests turning toward one of these.

- Face **{{shengQiDir}}** for the active, ambitious cooking, the long preparation, the dish that needs a push.
- Face **{{tianYiDir}}** for the steady, restorative everyday cooking.

Hold this lightly. The command position, seeing the door, outranks the facing direction every time. Where the two cannot both be satisfied, keep the door in view and let the favourable facing go. The direction is a fine adjustment you reach for once the larger moves are already in place, and only where the kitchen actually lets you choose.

There is one more thing your directions can tell you, about the room as a whole. A kitchen sits in some fixed compass sector of your home, and that sector is the same for everyone, but which of *your* directions falls there is yours alone. Check it: if the part of your home the kitchen occupies lines up with one of your four supportive directions ({{shengQiDir}}, {{tianYiDir}}, {{yanNianDir}}, or {{fuWeiDir}}), the tradition reads the room as sitting well for you. If it falls on one of your four to handle with care ({{huoHaiDir}}, {{wuGuiDir}}, {{liuShaDir}}, or {{jueMingDir}}), nothing is wrong, simply keep the room especially clean, clear, and in good order, which is what the kitchen reading asks of you regardless.

## Clear counters, clear flow

The last instruction is the simplest and the one most people feel fastest. The tradition reads a kitchen buried under appliances, mail, and unwashed dishes as a stagnant room, no matter how good the stove or the layout. A working kitchen needs working space.

- **Keep clear counter near the stove.** A stretch of empty surface beside the cooktop, enough to actually prep on, is the minimum the room needs to do its job. Everything that does not earn its place there goes elsewhere.
- **Let the cook move.** Sink, stove, and refrigerator within an easy reach of one another, with no through-traffic cutting across the cooking space. The room should let you turn from one to the next without an obstacle course.
- **Put sharp things away.** The tradition is consistent about blades left out in open view; keep knives in a block or a drawer rather than standing on the counter.
- **Keep the bin closed and the drains clean.** Both read as the room handling its waste rather than holding it.

A clean, clear, well-ordered kitchen with the stove cared for, the cook able to see the door, and fire and water given a little room from each other is, in this tradition, a kitchen doing what a kitchen is for. None of it is a guarantee of anything. It is a set of small, sensible decisions you can make about the room where the home is fed, and most of them you can start on tonight.
